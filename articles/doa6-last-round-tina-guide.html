DOA6 Last Round Tina Guide: Command Throws, Combos, Giant Swing & Complete Strategy

Tina Armstrong — the wrestling superstar, the daughter of Bass, the undisputed queen of grappling in Dead or Alive 6 Last Round — is one of the most explosive, accessible, and psychologically devastating characters in the entire roster. Unlike rushdown characters who chip away with blockstrings or zoners who control space from a distance, Tina operates on a single, terrifying principle: every interaction is a 50/50 between eating a strike combo or getting slammed for 70+ damage. Her game plan is simple to understand but devastating to face — condition the opponent to block with safe, plus-on-block strikes, then punish their passivity with the legendary Giant Swing. This guide covers everything from basic frame trap setups to advanced command throw vortex sequences, providing the complete path from your first 41236T input to tournament-ready Tina domination.

Tina Overview: Playstyle, Strengths & Weaknesses

Tina is a grappler-striker hybrid who leans heavily into the grappler side of the archetype. Her game plan is built around two core systems: command throws (41236T Giant Swing, 63214T Spinning Giant Swing, 46T Running Powerbomb — each dealing 65-75 damage), and plus-on-block strikes (6P at +2, 66K at +2, 3K at +1) that frame-trap the opponent into holding block, setting up the throw. Unlike pure grapplers like Bass who need to work hard to get into throw range, Tina has the tools to force her way in and keep herself there.

Strengths

  • Devastating throw damage: A single Giant Swing on Hi-Counter deals roughly 105 damage — more than many full combo routes. Land two throws per round and you win.
  • Plus-on-block frame traps: 6P (+2) and 66K (+2) create guaranteed frame advantage that forces the opponent into a strike-throw guess. No other grappler has this many plus-on-block options.
  • Simple, effective game plan: Tina's strategy is easy to understand: strike until they block, throw when they block. You don't need complex stance trees or teleport cross-ups — just solid conditioning and timing.
  • Strong okizeme: After every air throw ender (426T), Tina lands with frame advantage directly on top of the opponent. This sets up immediate command throw pressure on wake-up — the opponent must guess correctly while getting up.
  • Ring-out potential: The Giant Swing and Spinning Giant Swing have excellent carry distance. Near a danger zone or stage edge, a well-placed throw can end the round instantly.

Weaknesses

  • Short throw range: Tina's command throws have the shortest range of all DOA6 grapplers. You must be point-blank — literally touching the opponent — for 41236T to connect. A single backdash defeats all her throw attempts.
  • Poor tracking: Her main pokes (6P, 3K, 66K) are all linear. A well-timed sidestep beats her entire neutral game and leaves her vulnerable during the recovery frames.
  • Slow approach: Tina's primary gap-closer (66K) is i18. Against characters with strong keep-out tools (Ayane, Hayabusa, Jann Lee), getting into throw range requires patience and clever use of forward movement.
  • Throw-break vulnerability: Her Giant Swing (41236T) is broken with a 6T input, and her Spinning Giant Swing (63214T) is broken with a 4T input. Opponents who learn to fuzzy-break can severely reduce her damage output.
PRO TIP: Tina's biggest weakness is the opponent who never stops pressing buttons. If your opponent mashes after every blocked interaction, your command throw attempts will get stuffed every time. The solution is simple but requires discipline: strike more than you throw. Show the opponent 6P → 6P frame traps, 3K pokes, and 33P launchers 3-4 times for every one Giant Swing attempt. By the time you go for the throw, the opponent should be conditioned to freeze — and that's when Tina's damage output becomes truly terrifying.

The 6P Frame Trap: Tina's Bread and Butter

6P is +2 on block. This means after your 6P is blocked, your next button comes out 2 frames earlier than the opponent's fastest normal. The basic frame trap sequence for Tina is:

  1. 6P (blocked) → 6P: The second 6P beats any 10-frame or slower move. If the opponent presses a button, they eat a counter-hit. If they block, you are +2 again and the cycle continues. This is how you establish the strike threat.
  2. 6P (blocked) → 41236T (Giant Swing): After showing the 6P frame trap 3-4 times, the opponent will respect it and hold block. Buffer the Giant Swing during the blockstun — when they recover, the throw is already active and catches them for 70 damage.
  3. 6P (blocked) → 33P (launcher): If the opponent starts fuzzy-breaking (rapidly alternating between block and throw break), a delayed 33P will catch them in the transition frames and launch for a full combo. This punishes the most common advanced defense against Tina.
  4. 6P (blocked) → micro-dash → 6P: If the opponent backdashes after blocking 6P (which beats the Giant Swing), a micro-dash closes the gap and 6P catches them during the backdash recovery. This is how you keep them from escaping the vortex.
DRILL THIS: Set the Training Mode dummy to "Guard All" and "Attack after Guarding" with a 10-frame jab. Practice the 6P → 6P frame trap 50 times on each side. You should counter-hit the dummy on every successful repetition. Once this is automatic, practice 6P → 41236T — input the half-circle during the blockstun of your 6P so the Giant Swing activates the instant the dummy recovers. This single drill will improve your Tina pressure more than any combo practice.

The Giant Swing & Command Throw Arsenal: Input, Setups & Mind Games

Tina has the most versatile command throw toolkit of any grappler in DOA6 Last Round. While Bass has higher individual throw damage and Bayman has more throw setups, Tina's three command throws cover every defensive option the opponent can choose — and at 65-75 damage each, landing just two per round wins the match.

Mastering the Giant Swing (41236T)

The half-circle forward motion is the same input used for Ryu Hayabusa's Izuna Drop, and the execution tips are identical:

  1. Start at 4 (back): Press and hold the directional input toward the back direction. In a match, you will often be blocking before throwing, so starting at 4 is natural.
  2. Roll to 1 (down-back): Without lifting your thumb or stick, slide from 4 to 1 in one smooth motion.
  3. Roll to 2 (down): Continue sliding from 1 to 2.
  4. Roll to 3 (down-forward): Continue from 2 to 3 — keep the motion smooth and continuous.
  5. Finish at 6 (forward) + T: As you reach 6, press Throw. The motion should be one fluid half-circle, not five separate directional presses.

The most common error is pressing T before reaching 6 — this gives you a standard 4T or 1T throw instead of the Giant Swing. Go into Training Mode with input display turned on. The display should show a clean 4→1→2→3→6 arc. Clean half-circle = consistent Giant Swing.

Practical Giant Swing Setups

The Giant Swing is not a move you throw out randomly — it must be set up through conditioning. Here are the four most reliable setups:

  1. The Frame Trap Setup (6P on block → Giant Swing): After showing 6P → 6P frame traps 3-4 times, the opponent will block your 6P expecting another strike. Buffer 41236T during the blockstun. When they recover, the Giant Swing has already started and catches them. This is your bread-and-butter throw setup.
  2. The 66K Approach Setup (66K on block → Giant Swing): 66K is +2 on block, just like 6P. The running knee visually commits Tina forward, and most opponents freeze after blocking it because they expect a follow-up strike. Buffer the Giant Swing during the 66K blockstun. This setup works especially well at mid-range because the 66K closes the distance and the throw is immediately active after.
  3. The Side-Switch Mix-Up (63214T instead of 41236T): When the opponent has started breaking your Giant Swing consistently with a 6T input, switch to the Spinning Giant Swing. The 63214T uses a 4T break direction, which beats players who are auto-piloting 6T breaks. The side switch also puts their back to the wall or danger zone, setting up stage-specific damage.
  4. The Wake-Up Throw (dash → Giant Swing on opponent's wake-up): After landing any air throw ender (426T), dash forward as the opponent wakes up. If they wake up blocking (which most players do against Tina because they fear her okizeme strikes), the Giant Swing catches them. If they wake-up kick, you have time to block and punish. This setup turns every combo ender into a potential second throw.
THE HI-COUNTER GIANT SWING: The single most devastating interaction in Tina's kit is a Hi-Counter Giant Swing — 105 damage, roughly 35% of a standard life bar. This happens when you throw an opponent during their hold recovery frames. The setup: after 6P on block, delay your Giant Swing input by roughly 15 frames instead of buffering it immediately. If the opponent panic-holds expecting a strike, your delayed throw lands during their hold recovery for Hi-Counter damage. Against opponents below B-Rank, this setup works almost every time because they instinctively hold when pressured. One Hi-Counter Giant Swing followed by a standard combo often ends the round.

The Command Throw Vortex: Strike-Throw Conditioning & Reset Loops

The Tina vortex is the core of her entire game plan. It is not a complex sequence of specific moves — it is a decision tree that loops the opponent through the same 50/50 guess over and over until they guess wrong and lose 70+ health. Understanding the vortex is the difference between a Tina who occasionally lands Giant Swings and a Tina who makes the opponent feel like every interaction is a lose-lose situation.

The Four-Step Vortex Cycle

Every Tina interaction follows this cycle:

Step 1 — Establish the Strike Threat: Start the match by pressing buttons. 6P, 3K, 6P → 6P frame trap. Show the opponent that if they don't block, they will eat counter-hit strikes and launcher combos. Do this at least 3-4 interactions before even thinking about a command throw. The goal is to make the opponent respect your strikes.

Step 2 — Call Out the Block: Once the opponent is blocking after your 6P and 66K, they are ready for the throw. Buffer 41236T during the blockstun of your next 6P. The Giant Swing catches them during their block recovery. This is the moment that defines Tina — the opponent realizes they can't just block their way out of her pressure.

Step 3 — Punish the Panic Response: After getting Giant Swinged once or twice, the opponent will change their behavior. They will do one of three things: (a) mash buttons after your 6P to beat the throw — counter this with the 6P → 6P frame trap; (b) backdash after your 6P to escape throw range — counter this with micro-dash → 6P or 66K to chase the backdash; (c) hold after your 6P to beat strikes — counter this with Hi-Counter Giant Swing for 105 damage. Every panic response has a hard punish.

Step 4 — Okizeme Reset: After any combo ending in the 426T air throw, Tina lands directly on top of the waking opponent. Dash forward to maintain point-blank range. The opponent must guess on wake-up: block (Giant Swing), wake-up kick (block and punish), or tech-roll (chase with 66K). If they guess wrong, the vortex resets and you start again from Step 1.

THE 70-30 RULE: The most common mistake new Tina players make is throwing too often. The ratio should be roughly 70% strikes, 30% throws. If you throw more than 40% of the time, the opponent will start mashing and you'll never get a throw off. The strikes are not just damage — they are the threat that makes the throw work. Without the strike threat, your throws are read like a book. Strike more than feels natural, and your throws will land more often.

Optimal Combo Routes: BnB, Wall Carry, Break Blow & Counter Hit

Tina's combo game is built around two launchers — 33P (i16, primary) and 8P (i17, alternate) — with optimized routes for every situation. Her combos are shorter than rushdown characters but hit harder, and the air throw ender (426T) provides excellent okizeme positioning.

Universal BnB (Normal Hit, Midscreen)

Route: 33P → 6PP → 426T

Damage: 72

This is your go-to combo. It works from any 33P launch at any range, has forgiving timing, and the 426T air throw ender slams the opponent directly at Tina's feet for immediate okizeme. Practice this until it is automatic — it is the foundation of Tina's combo game.

Counter Hit / Hi-Counter Hit Extension

Route: 33P (Counter Hit) → 6K → 6PP → 426T

Damage: 84 (CH) / 100 (HCH)

On Counter Hit, the 33P launches higher, creating enough air time for an additional 6K before the standard 6PP → 426T ender. The extra 6K adds 12-28 damage depending on the hit level. This is the route you use when you score a counter-hit confirm — recognizing the higher launch and extending the combo is a key skill.

Optimal Wall Carry

Route: 33P → 6PP → 66K → 4K → 426T

Damage: 78 (carries ~70% of stage length)

The 66K after 6PP carries significantly farther than ending immediately. The 4K at the end activates a wall splat if the opponent touches the wall during the kick. On stages with close walls (The Throwdown, Lost Paradise), this combo will wall-splat from nearly mid-stage.

Wall Splat Combo

Route: 33P → 6PP → 4K (Wall Splat) → 6PP → 426T

Damage: 88

When 4K wall-splats, you have time for a second 6PP before the air throw ender. The wall splat adds roughly 16 extra damage compared to the standard BnB. On stages with dangerous walls (The Muscle, Forbidden Fortune), this is your go-to combo.

Break Blow Extended Combo

Route: 33P → 6PP → Break Blow → 66K → 4K (Wall Splat) → 6PP → 426T

Damage: 115 (midscreen), 130 (near wall)

This is your maximum-damage route with full Break Gauge. The Break Blow must be input immediately after 6PP connects — any delay and the opponent will tech out. At 130 damage near a wall, this combo deletes roughly 45% of a standard life bar. Save your Break Gauge for these guaranteed combo extensions rather than using Break Blows raw in neutral.

8P Launcher Combo

Route: 8P → 6PP → 426T

Damage: 68

8P is slightly slower than 33P (i17 vs i16) but safer on block (-8 vs -10). Use 8P when you want to fish for a launcher without the risk of eating a full punish if blocked. The combo route is identical to the BnB after the launch.

Neutral & Spacing: Approach Tools, Pokes & The Mid-Range Game

Tina's neutral game is built around closing the distance safely and maintaining point-blank range once she is in. She lacks the long-range tools of zoners, so every Tina player must learn how to navigate the dangerous space between full-screen and throw range where she cannot threaten command throws.

The Approach Toolkit

At mid-range (roughly 2-3 character widths apart), Tina has three primary approach tools:

  • 66K (i18, +2 on block): Your best approach tool. The running knee covers distance quickly and leaves you at +2 if blocked — meaning you can immediately start the 6P frame trap or Giant Swing vortex from a blocked 66K. The speed (i18) means it is reactable, so use it when the opponent is committed to a slow move or recovering from a whiff. Don't spam 66K — a good opponent will sidestep it on reaction and launch-punish you.
  • Dash → 6P (effectively i22-i25): A single forward dash into 6P covers roughly 3 character widths. It is slower than 66K but less telegraphed — the opponent sees a dash, not a running knee startup. Mix dashes with 66K to keep your approach unpredictable.
  • 46T (Running Powerbomb, i15): This command throw has roughly 1.5x the range of the Giant Swing. When you are just outside 41236T range but close enough that the opponent is blocking, 46T catches them off-guard. The risk is that at i15, a mashing opponent will stuff it — use 46T only when you've conditioned the opponent to block at mid-range.

Maintaining Point-Blank Range

Once Tina is in throw range, her goal is to never let the opponent escape. Opponents will try to backdash after every blocked interaction to escape the Giant Swing threat. Your counters:

  • Micro-dash → 6P: A tiny forward dash cancels the backdash distance and 6P catches the opponent during backdash recovery. This single technique — micro-dash 6P on opponent backdash — is what separates good Tinas from great ones.
  • 66K on backdash reaction: If the opponent backdashes at predictable moments (e.g., always after blocking 3K), 66K will catch them before the backdash completes.
  • Forward walk: Between interactions, hold forward. Tina walks forward faster than most characters walk backward. If the opponent is walking backward to create space, you are closing the distance for free.
THE CLOSE-RANGE COMMITMENT: Tina's entire game plan requires point-blank range. If the opponent is more than one character width away, you have zero throw threat and your strike game is limited to 3K pokes. The #1 skill for Tina players is not combo execution or throw inputs — it is range management. Every moment you are not in throw range is a moment you are losing. Practice movement: dash-blocking through projectiles and long-range pokes, micro-dashing after blocked strikes to prevent escape, and forward-walking between interactions. The best Tinas make the opponent feel claustrophobic — no matter how much they backdash, Tina is always right in their face.

Matchup Strategy: How Tina Beats Every Archetype

Tina's approach changes based on the opponent's character archetype. Here is the high-level game plan against each of the four major DOA6 archetypes.

vs. Rushdown (Kasumi, Christie, Hitomi, Jann Lee)

Rushdown characters want to suffocate you with plus-on-block pressure and never let you establish your own offense. Tina's counter-strategy relies on interrupting their pressure with 6P and punishing their over-commitment with command throws. Rushdown characters throw out a lot of buttons — and every button they throw is a window where they are not blocking. Use 6P to challenge after their negative-on-block strings (most rushdown strings are -1 to -3). When you score a counter-hit 6P or 3K, go immediately into the vortex. The key: rushdown players are used to being the aggressor and often panic-hold when they are suddenly on defense. One blocked 6P at +2 is all you need to start the Giant Swing mind game.

vs. Grapplers (Bass, Bayman, Leon)

The grappler mirror is a test of who can establish their throw threat first. Against other grapplers, Tina's advantage is her superior frame data. Bass's fastest mid is i13; Bayman's is i12. Tina's i11 6P beats both. Use 6P to challenge after their blocked strikes — if they try to command throw, your 6P stuffs it. If they block, the +2 advantage sets up your own Giant Swing. The grappler mirror is fundamentally about who gets to be +2 first — and Tina wins that race against every other grappler. One additional note: grapplers tend to hold less than other archetypes because they want to throw, not hold. This means your Hi-Counter Giant Swing setup (delayed throw on hold recovery) is less effective — instead, throw immediately during their blockstun for standard damage.

vs. Setplay/Zoners (Ayane, Nyotengu, Phase 4, Marie Rose)

Setplay characters want to keep you at range and force you to navigate their traps. Against these characters, Tina's game plan is patience and explosive gap-closing. Walk forward while blocking. When you see an opening (a whiffed long-range attack, a stance transition, a backdash to create distance), commit to 66K to close the gap. Once you are in, do not let them escape — micro-dash 6P on every backdash, 66K on every retreat attempt. The key to beating zoners with Tina is accepting that you will take some chip damage on the way in. Don't panic, don't start mashing, don't try to out-zone them. Close the distance methodically, and once you are in throw range, make your one opportunity count — a single Giant Swing into okizeme vortex can take 50% of their health.

vs. Hybrid/All-Rounders (Rig, Diego, Kokoro, Brad Wong)

Hybrid characters do a bit of everything, which means they have no overwhelming strength for you to shut down — but also no glaring weakness for you to exploit. Against hybrids, play standard Tina offense: 6P frame traps, 3K pokes, 66K approaches, and Giant Swing when they are conditioned. The key to beating hybrids is discipline. Hybrids win by exploiting impatience — they poke you, they move around, they make you feel like you need to force something. Don't take the bait. Play patient, maintain close range, and trust that two Giant Swings and one combo per round wins the match. You don't need to outplay the hybrid's entire toolset — you just need to land your win conditions more consistently than they land theirs.

The Tina Learning Path: Beginner to Competitive

Learning Tina is a four-phase journey from your first half-circle input to a complete competitive grappler. Here is the structured progression.

Phase 1: The Fundamentals (Hours 1-10)

Goals: Consistent Giant Swing input (41236T) on both sides. BnB combo (33P → 6PP → 426T) on P1 and P2. 6P frame trap (6P on block → 6P counter-hit). Basic vortex structure (strike → strike → throw).

Practice Routine: 15 minutes of Giant Swing input drills (aim for 90% consistency on both sides). 10 minutes of BnB combo repetition (aim for 10 in a row without dropping). 5 minutes of 6P frame trap against Training Mode dummy. Then play 5-10 casual matches, focusing only on landing the Giant Swing and the BnB combo. Don't worry about winning — the goal is execution under live pressure.

Rank Expectation: F-Rank to D-Rank. At this level, opponents hold constantly and have never encountered a grappler who punishes blocking. The Giant Swing alone will win you matches.

Phase 2: The Vortex (Hours 10-30)

Goals: Fluent vortex cycling (strike → throw → strike → throw). 66K approach into vortex. Spinning Giant Swing (63214T) as a side-switch mix-up. Low throw (2T) to punish crouching. Basic okizeme pressure after 426T air throw.

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of vortex drills (set dummy to random guard — practice cycling between 6P and 41236T based on dummy behavior). 10 minutes of 66K → vortex practice. 5 minutes of okizeme drills (record dummy doing various wake-up options, practice your responses). Then 10 ranked matches, with the goal of landing at least 3 command throws per match.

Rank Expectation: D-Rank to C+. Opponents at this level can block basic strings but cannot handle the strike-throw 50/50 because they have never faced a grappler who cycles options consciously.

Phase 3: Counter-Adaptation (Hours 30-60)

Goals: Hi-Counter Giant Swing setups (delayed throw to punish holds). Side-switch conditioning (opponent breaking 6T → switch to 63214T). Wall carry optimization. Matchup-specific game plans for your 5 most common opponents. Okizeme full-coverage (dash → throw, dash → block, dash → 2T).

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of Hi-Counter setup drills (dummy set to hold after blocking — practice delayed 41236T timing). 10 minutes of wall combo optimization on the 5 most common stages. 5 minutes of matchup labbing (record your problem matchup's top strings and practice your counterplay). Then ranked grind with a focus on adapting — after every match, note how the opponent defended against your throws and practice that specific counter for 5 minutes before the next match.

Rank Expectation: C+ to B+. At this level, opponents start breaking throws and mashing intelligently. Your ability to cycle between strike and throw based on their adaptation — rather than on a fixed pattern — is what wins matches.

Phase 4: The Complete Tina (Hours 60+)

Goals: Full vortex adaptation (strike/throw/sidestep coverage against every defensive option). Optimal Break Blow routing from any confirm. Complete matchup knowledge against the full roster. Micro-dash spacing mastery to maintain throw range against any backdash timing.

Practice Routine: 70% ranked matches, 30% labbing. The lab work at this phase is pure matchup study — after every session, spend 15 minutes labbing the one character who gave you the most trouble. Focus on finding one specific interaction where you can improve (e.g., "I'm letting Ayane backdash after my 66K — I need to micro-dash 6P faster").

Rank Expectation: B+ to S-Rank. A complete Tina at this level has become a psychological nightmare — the opponent knows the Giant Swing is coming, knows they need to strike or backdash to avoid it, but can never predict exactly when. When your conditioning is so deep that the opponent hesitates for even one frame, Tina wins.

THE TINA MINDSET: Tina is not a character who wins through complex execution or flashy combos. She wins through psychological pressure. Your goal as a Tina player is not to out-execute the opponent — it is to make them feel like every defensive choice is wrong. When they block, they get thrown. When they strike, they get counter-hit. When they backdash, you chase them. When they hold, you delay and Hi-Counter them. A great Tina player doesn't just beat the opponent's character — they break the opponent's confidence. If your opponent leaves the match thinking "I had no idea what to do," you have played Tina correctly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How do you perform the Giant Swing with Tina in DOA6?
The Giant Swing (41236T) is Tina's signature command throw executed with a half-circle forward motion followed by Throw. It deals 70 base damage and has incredible ring-out potential — a Giant Swing near the edge of any stage with danger zones or walls often ends the round outright. The input must be clean: start at 4 (back), roll through 1, 2, 3, and press T at 6 (forward). Tina also has the spinning Giant Swing variant (63214T) for 75 damage that switches sides with the opponent. Practice both variants in Training Mode with input display on — a clean half-circle motion is the difference between a whiffed throw and a match-winning Giant Swing. The most reliable setup is after a blocked 6P at +2 — buffer 41236T during the blockstun and release T as the opponent recovers. If they hold or block, the Giant Swing catches them every time.
Q: Is Tina good for beginners in DOA6?
Tina is an excellent character for beginners who want to learn the grappler playstyle. She has the lowest execution barrier of all the DOA6 grapplers — her command throws use simple half-circle and quarter-circle motions rather than the 360-degree inputs required by Bass or the stance-cancel sequences of Bayman. Her strike game is straightforward (6P, 3K, 4K, 66K) and her combo routes are short and high-damage. Q: How do I counter Tina in DOA6?
Countering Tina requires understanding that her entire game plan collapses if you refuse to block passively. Tina's command throws (41236T, 63214T, 46T) all have roughly 12-frame startup — meaning if you are pressing buttons at advantage, your strikes will beat her throw attempts every time. The correct defensive approach against Tina is: (1) Stay active — after blocking her 6P, challenge with your fastest mid (i10-i11). If she tries to Giant Swing, your strike wins. (3) Sidestep frequently — Tina's main pokes (6P, 3K, 66K) have poor tracking, and sidestepping beats both her strikes and her throw attempts. (4) Break her Giant Swing attempts — her Giant Swing is technically a 6T throw and can be broken with 6T input during the throw window. However, her 63214T Spinning Giant Swing requires a 4T break, so you must watch which throw she favors. Tina's key weakness is her reliance on conditioning — if you never allow yourself to be conditioned into blocking, she never gets to start her vortex. Stay aggressive, stay mobile, and make her prove she can hit you before you respect the throw.
