DOA6 Last Round Bayman Guide: Sambo Throws, Combos, Armor & Complete Strategy

Bayman — the armored Sambo specialist, the Russian tank, the most punishing defensive grappler in Dead or Alive 6 Last Round — is a character who thrives on patience, reads, and the opponent's mistakes. Where Tina rushes in with frame traps and Giant Swings, Bayman sits back, absorbs incoming attacks with his unique armor property, and then delivers devastating command throws that drain half the opponent's health bar. He is not flashy. He is not fast. But when Bayman reads you correctly — when he armors through your best string and slams you with a 72-damage Death Valley Driver — you feel it in your bones. This guide covers everything from his armor mechanics to his 360-degree command throw setups, optimal combo routes, wall carry, okizeme pressure, and every matchup in the roster, providing the complete path from your first armored read to tournament-level Bayman dominance.

1. Bayman Overview: Playstyle, Strengths & Weaknesses

Bayman occupies a unique niche in the DOA6 roster as the game's premier defensive grappler. While Tina and Bass are grapplers who want to rush in and throw, Bayman's design philosophy is inverted — he wants the opponent to come to him. His game plan revolves around three pillars: absorbing attacks with his armor property, controlling mid-range space with superior pokes, and punishing predictable offense with high-damage command throws. Bayman does not need to pressure the opponent into blocking — he needs the opponent to think they can attack him safely. Every blocked string, every whiffed poke, every predictable wake-up kick is an opportunity for Bayman to convert a defensive read into 60-70+ damage.

Strengths

  • Armor Property: Unique defensive mechanic that lets Bayman absorb hits and continue his own attack — the only character in DOA6 with this ability.
  • Command Throw Damage: Bayman's throws deal some of the highest raw damage in the game — the 360T Giant Swing does 70 damage, the Death Valley Driver does 72, and the Power Slam does 68. All three lead to excellent okizeme positioning.
  • Counter-Hit Monsters: Many of Bayman's key moves have amplified properties on Counter Hit, turning simple pokes into full combo starters or massive damage.
  • Wall Carry & Stage Abuse: Bayman's combo routes carry well and his throw enders slam opponents into walls and danger zones for massive bonus damage.
  • Okizeme Dominance: After every command throw or combo, Bayman is positioned perfectly for a wake-up vortex — the opponent must guess between standing throw, low throw, or armored attack on every wake-up.
  • Psychological Pressure: The mere threat of Bayman's armor changes how opponents approach their offense. Many players become overly cautious, which feeds Bayman's throw game.

Weaknesses

  • Slow Approach: Bayman's forward movement and approach tools are among the slowest in the roster. Against zoners and keep-out characters, he can struggle to close distance.
  • Execution Barrier: The 360-degree Giant Swing (360T) requires a full rotation input that is significantly harder to execute consistently than Tina's half-circle throws.
  • Armor Vulnerability: While armored, Bayman still takes reduced damage. Multi-hit strings and Break Blows can break through armor and punish Bayman for committing to it.
  • Poor From Behind: When Bayman is losing and the opponent has a life lead, his defensive playstyle becomes a liability — the opponent can simply refuse to approach and run the clock.
  • String Dependency: Without counter-hit confirms, Bayman's combo damage from neutral is moderate. He relies on reads more than flowchart offense.

3. The Armor System: Mechanics, Setups & When to Absorb

Bayman's armor is his most unique and defining characteristic — a defensive mechanic that no other character in DOA6 possesses. Understanding armor is understanding Bayman. It transforms him from a slow, punishable grappler into a character who can turn the opponent's offense against them in the most devastating way possible.

How Armor Works

When Bayman activates his armor stance through specific move inputs, he enters a state where he absorbs the next incoming hit without entering hitstun. He still takes reduced damage (roughly 50% of the original hit's damage), but he continues executing his own attack uninterrupted. This means: if the opponent throws a mid punch and Bayman armors through it, Bayman's own armored attack connects during the opponent's recovery frames, giving him a full combo or command throw opportunity.

When to Use Armor

Armor is most effective in specific situations:

  • Against predictable pressure strings: When the opponent uses the same string repeatedly (e.g., a two-hit string into a third predictable hit), armor the third hit and punish.
  • On wake-up: When the opponent is pressuring you with meaty attacks on your wake-up, armor through the meaty and counter-throw or strike.
  • Against slow, committed moves: Armor is most rewarding against moves with long recovery. Armoring through a heavy attack gives Bayman the maximum punish window.
  • In the mid-range neutral: When the opponent is poking with 3K or similar mid-range tools, armor through the poke and close distance for a command throw.

When NOT to Use Armor

  • Against multi-hit strings: Armor absorbs one hit. If the opponent's string has three rapid hits, the first gets absorbed but the second and third connect normally, often leading to a full combo against Bayman.
  • Against Break Blows: Break Blow attacks pierce armor entirely. Never armor against an opponent with full Break Blow meter.
  • Against throws: Armor does not protect against throws. If the opponent reads your armor, they can throw you out of it for a Hi-Counter throw (massive damage).
  • When low on health: Armor still deals reduced damage to Bayman. If you are at critical health, even the reduced damage from an armored hit can kill you.
ARMOR MIND GAME: The true power of Bayman's armor is not the armor itself — it is the threat of armor. Once the opponent knows you will armor through their strings, they start hesitating. They delay their pressure, they stop committing to follow-ups, they start throwing instead of striking. All of this hesitation feeds directly into Bayman's command throw game. The best Bayman players use armor sparingly — once or twice per match to establish the threat — and then ride the opponent's fear for the rest of the set. Armor is a psychological weapon as much as a mechanical one.

4. Command Throw Arsenal: 360T, Power Slam, Death Valley Driver & More

Bayman's command throws are his primary damage source. Unlike Tina, whose command throws are mostly half-circle motions, Bayman has a mix of half-circle and 360-degree inputs that cover different ranges and situations. Mastering the full throw arsenal — and knowing which throw to use in each scenario — is what separates a good Bayman from a terrifying one.

41236T — Power Slam (68 damage)

Bayman's fastest and most reliable command throw. The half-circle forward motion is straightforward, and the 68 damage is nothing to scoff at. Use this as your primary throw from close range after frame traps — 6P blocked at +1, buffer 41236T, and the Power Slam catches anyone who hesitated for even a frame. The Power Slam slams the opponent directly in front of Bayman, giving excellent okizeme positioning for the wake-up vortex.

63214T — Death Valley Driver (72 damage)

The Death Valley Driver is Bayman's highest-damage standard command throw at 72 damage, and it switches sides with the opponent. The half-circle back input is slightly slower to buffer than the Power Slam, making it better as a hard read rather than a reaction throw. The side switch is the real value — after a Death Valley Driver, the opponent is now cornered against the wall, and Bayman's wall combo damage skyrockets. Use this when your back is near a wall: throw them, switch sides, and now they are cornered with Bayman's devastating wall okizeme.

360T — Giant Swing (70 damage)

The 360-degree Giant Swing is Bayman's most technically demanding but most rewarding command throw. The full rotation input (start at any direction, complete the circle, press T) has the longest range of all Bayman's throws, catching opponents who think they are safe at mid-range. The 70 damage is excellent, and the Giant Swing sends the opponent tumbling across the stage, often into walls or danger zones for additional damage. The execution barrier is real — practice the 360 input until it is muscle memory. A common technique is to buffer the rotation during a dash or during the recovery of a blocked poke, so the 360T comes out seamlessly.

Throw Mix-Up Strategy

Bayman's throw game becomes truly dangerous when you vary which throw you use:

  • After 6P (blocked, +1): Use 41236T (Power Slam) — it is the fastest to buffer and catches the most opponents.
  • After 66K (blocked, +1): Use 360T (Giant Swing) — the extra range of the 360 input catches opponents who try to backdash after blocking the knee.
  • When back is to a wall: Use 63214T (Death Valley Driver) — the side switch corners them against the wall for devastating follow-ups.
  • Against crouching opponents: Use 2T (low throw) — catches duckers for 45 damage and resets to neutral.
THROW BREAK MIND GAME: Each of Bayman's throws has a different break input — 41236T breaks with 6T, 63214T breaks with 4T, and 360T breaks with 6T. If the opponent learns to break your Power Slam consistently, switch to the Death Valley Driver (4T break) — they will be pressing the wrong break input and eat 72 damage. Varying your throws based on which break the opponent is looking for adds an extra layer of reads to every interaction.

5. Optimal Combo Routes: BnB, Wall Carry, Break Blow & Counter Hit

Bayman's combo game is built for raw damage. Unlike Tina's combos, which are shorter and throw-end-focused, Bayman's combos deal substantial damage from strikes alone, making him less reliant on landing a throw to win rounds. The combo structure follows a consistent pattern: launcher → filler → ender → okizeme.

Universal BnB

33P → 6PP → 4K → 3K — 75 damage. This is your bread and butter from any launcher confirm. The 33P launches, 6PP is the standard filler, 4K extends the juggle, and 3K is the ender that leaves the opponent grounded at Bayman's ideal okizeme range. Practice this until it is automatic — you should be able to convert any stray 33P hit into 75 damage without thinking.

Wall Carry Route

33P → 6PP → 66K → 4K → 3K — 78 damage. The added 66K before the 4K extends the carry distance, pushing the opponent approximately 65% across the stage. Use this route when the opponent is between you and a wall — the extra carry often gets you close enough for a wall splat on the 4K.

Wall Combo

33P → 6PP → 4K (wall splat) → 6PP → 6T — 90 damage. When near a wall, 4K causes a wall splat that allows an additional 6PP → 6T sequence. The 6T ender is Bayman's regular throw ender that slams the opponent into the wall for the final hit. This is Bayman's highest consistent damage route and should be your priority whenever you are near a wall.

Break Blow Extension

33P → 6PP → Break Blow → 66K → 4K (wall splat) → 6PP → 6T — 118 damage. With full Break Blow meter, extend the combo after 6PP with the Break Blow, then continue with wall carry into wall combo. This is Bayman's highest damage combo and can swing an entire round. The Break Blow must be timed immediately after the last hit of 6PP — practice the timing in Training Mode.

Counter Hit Confirm

33P (CH) → 6K → 6PP → 4K → 3K — 88 damage. On Counter Hit, 33P launches higher, giving you time to add a 6K before the standard 6PP filler. This extra hit adds 13 damage to the BnB and is the reason Bayman players fish for Counter Hit 33P at mid-range. The 88 damage from a single Counter Hit confirm is enough to decide a round.

Hi-Counter Throw Combos

Bayman's command throws deal Hi-Counter damage when the opponent is caught during a hold or strike attempt. A Hi-Counter 360T deals approximately 90 damage, and a Hi-Counter Death Valley Driver deals 95 damage. These are round-ending punishes — if you read the opponent holding, the Hi-Counter throw essentially wins the round outright.

6. Neutral & Spacing: The Defensive Mid-Range Game

Bayman's neutral game is defined by his position at mid-range — close enough to threaten with 3K pokes and 66K approaches, far enough to react to the opponent's forward movement with armor or command throws. Bayman does not want to be in the opponent's face constantly; he wants to hover at the edge of his effective range, waiting for the opponent to overcommit.

The Mid-Range Stance

Bayman's ideal positioning is approximately two character lengths away from the opponent. At this range, his 3K poke covers the space in front of him, his 66K can close distance quickly, and his 360T Giant Swing can catch opponents who try to approach. From this position, Bayman wants to:

  • Poke with 3K to check the opponent's forward movement. On hit, 3K gives slight advantage, letting Bayman dash in for 6P pressure.
  • Armor through predictable approaches when the opponent commits to a mid-range attack. Read the timing and absorb the hit, then punish with a command throw.
  • Backdash and whiff punish when the opponent reaches with a long-range move. Bayman's backdash is decent, and a whiffed move at mid-range is a free 33P launch into 75+ damage.
  • Dash in with 66K when the opponent hesitates. 66K is +1 on block, giving Bayman the close-range frame advantage he needs to start the throw vortex.

Approach Options

When Bayman needs to close distance, his options are more limited than faster characters, but they are effective:

  • 66K (Running Knee): +1 on block, i17 startup. Your primary approach tool. Dash in, 66K, and you are at frame advantage for your next action.
  • Dash → 6P: Simple but effective. Dash forward and immediately 6P — the i12 mid at +1 on block starts the frame trap sequence.
  • Dash → 360T: High-risk, high-reward. If the opponent does not challenge your dash, the 360T Giant Swing catches them at range. If they do challenge, you eat a combo.
  • Walk forward → block: The safest approach. Walk forward slowly, block whatever the opponent throws, and then use the frame advantage from their minus move to start your pressure.

7. Okizeme & Wake-Up Pressure: Post-Throw Vortex

Bayman's okizeme game is what makes his command throws so devastating. After every command throw or combo ender, Bayman is positioned perfectly to pressure the opponent's wake-up. The opponent must choose between several options, and Bayman has an answer for each one.

8. Matchup Guide: Strategies Against Every Archetype

Bayman's matchups vary significantly based on the opponent's playstyle archetype. Understanding how to adjust your game plan against each type is essential for consistent tournament performance.

vs. Rushdown Characters (Kasumi, Hayabusa, Christie)

Rushdown characters want to pressure Bayman with fast strings and constant offense. This is actually one of Bayman's best matchups — armor is devastating against predictable rushdown pressure. Let them start their strings, armor through the predictable follow-up, and punish with a command throw. Use 6P to interrupt their pressure when you have frame advantage, and punish their minus-on-block strings with 41236T. The key is patience: do not try to out-pressure a rushdown character. Let them come to you, absorb their offense, and throw them for it.

vs. Zoners (Jann Lee, Helena, Kokoro)

Zoners want to keep Bayman at long range and chip away with safe pokes. This is Bayman's worst matchup category because his approach tools are slow and zoners can backdash out of his throw range. The strategy is to walk forward patiently, block their pokes, and punish any overcommitment with 33P launches. Use 66K to close distance when they hesitate. Do not rush — a patient Bayman walking forward is scarier than a dashing one, because the opponent never knows when the 360T Giant Swing is coming.

vs. Grapplers (Tina, Bass)

Mirror and grappler matchups are pure reads. Both players want to throw each other, so the game becomes about who makes the first mistake. Break their command throws aggressively — learn Tina's 6T break and Bass's 6T break. Use armor sparingly against grapplers because they will throw you out of it for Hi-Counter damage. Focus on striking first with 6P and 3K, and only throw when you have a confirmed read. In the Tina matchup specifically, out-space her with 3K since her throw range is shorter than yours.

vs. Defensive Characters (Bass, Bayman mirror)

Against defensive opponents who refuse to approach, Bayman must become the aggressor — an unusual role for him. Walk forward, establish 6P pressure, and force the 50/50 at close range. The opponent wants you to overcommit to slow approaches; instead, take small steps forward with 6P and 3K pokes until you are in throw range. Patience wins these matchups — eventually the defensive player will try to poke, and Bayman's armor or Counter Hit 33P will punish them.

9. Training Mode Routine: From Beginner to Advanced

Phase 1: Foundations (Hours 0-10)

Goals: Consistent 360T input. Basic BnB combo (33P → 6PP → 4K → 3K). Understanding armor timing. Landing 41236T from close range.

Practice Routine: 15 minutes of 360T input practice with input display on — start with the full rotation slowly, then speed up until it comes out reliably. 10 minutes of BnB combo practice against a stationary dummy. 5 minutes of armor drills (record dummy doing a single hit, practice activating armor and punishing). Then 5 casual matches focusing only on landing one command throw per round.

Rank Expectation: F-Rank to D-Rank. At this stage, simply landing command throws against opponents who do not know the matchup will carry you.

Phase 2: Frame Trap Mastery (Hours 10-30)

Goals: Fluent 6P → throw cycling. 66K approach into frame trap. All three command throws accessible in real matches. Wall combo execution. Basic okizeme after throws.

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of frame trap drills (set dummy to random guard — practice cycling between 6P and 41236T based on dummy behavior). 10 minutes of 66K → throw practice. 10 minutes of wall combo optimization. Then 10 ranked matches, with the goal of landing at least 3 command throws per match and converting every wall splat into maximum damage.

Rank Expectation: D-Rank to C+. Opponents at this level can block basic strings but cannot handle the strike-throw 50/50 or the armor mind game.

Phase 3: Armor Mastery & Counter-Adaptation (Hours 30-60)

Goals: Armor reads in real matches. Hi-Counter throw setups. Side-switch conditioning with Death Valley Driver. Matchup-specific game plans for your 5 most common opponents. Full okizeme coverage.

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of armor drills (record dummy doing common strings from your problem matchups, practice armoring the right hit and punishing). 10 minutes of Hi-Counter setup drills (dummy set to hold after blocking — practice delayed 41236T timing). 5 minutes of matchup labbing. Then ranked grind with a focus on adapting — after every match, note how the opponent dealt with your armor and throws, and practice the counter for 5 minutes before the next match.

Rank Expectation: C+ to B+. At this level, opponents start breaking throws and respecting armor. Your ability to cycle between armor, throws, and strikes based on their adaptation is what wins matches.

Phase 4: The Complete Bayman (Hours 60+)

Goals: Full vortex adaptation. Optimal Break Blow routing from any confirm. Complete matchup knowledge. Micro-dash 360T from mid-range. Armor as a psychological threat rather than a mechanical crutch.

Practice Routine: 70% ranked matches, 30% labbing. The lab work at this phase is pure matchup study — after every session, spend 15 minutes labbing the one character who gave you the most trouble. Focus on one specific interaction where you can improve (e.g., "I'm letting Jann Lee zone me out — I need to walk forward and block his 6K more patiently").

Rank Expectation: B+ to S-Rank. A complete Bayman at this level is a fortress — the opponent knows the armor is coming, knows the throws are coming, but can never predict exactly when. When your defensive reads are so deep that the opponent becomes afraid to press buttons, Bayman wins.

THE BAYMAN MINDSET: Bayman is not a character who wins through speed or flashy combos. He wins through defensive reads and psychological warfare. Your goal as a Bayman player is to make the opponent feel like every offensive choice is wrong. When they attack, they get armored and thrown. When they block, they get thrown anyway. When they backdash, you chase with 360T. When they hesitate, you close distance with 66K and start the pressure. A great Bayman player does not just beat the opponent's character — they dismantle the opponent's confidence in their own offense. If your opponent leaves the match thinking "I couldn't do anything without getting punished," you have played Bayman correctly. Patience is not passive — it is the most aggressive thing Bayman can do.

10. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is Bayman's armor property in DOA6?
Bayman's armor is a unique defensive mechanic exclusive to his character in DOA6 Last Round. When Bayman activates his armor stance through specific move inputs, he gains super armor — meaning he can absorb one or more hits without being interrupted or entering hitstun. Unlike a hold or parry, armor does not nullify damage entirely; Bayman still takes reduced damage while armored, but he continues executing his own attack uninterrupted. This makes armor devastating for beating predictable pressure strings and frame traps. The key is timing: activate armor when the opponent is mid-string or committed to an attack, and Bayman's armored strike will connect during their recovery. Armor is best used against characters who rely on multi-hit strings to maintain pressure — against opponents who know to stagger their offense, you'll need to mix armor with standard defense and throws.
Q: Is Bayman good for beginners in DOA6?
Bayman is a solid choice for intermediate players but has a steeper learning curve than Tina for true beginners. His command throws require 360-degree and half-circle motions that demand cleaner execution than Tina's simpler quarter-circle and half-circle inputs. His armor mechanic adds another layer of complexity — you need to know when to armor through attacks versus when to block or throw. That said, Bayman's core game plan is straightforward: he is a defensive grappler who punishes predictable offense. If you already understand DOA6's strike-throw-hold triangle and can perform half-circle motions consistently, Bayman rewards patience and reads more than any other character. His damage output is massive — a single correct read can swing an entire round. For beginners who want to learn the grappler archetype, start with Tina first, then graduate to Bayman when you want deeper defensive options and higher skill ceiling.
Q: What are Bayman's best moves in DOA6?
Bayman's essential toolkit includes: 6P (i12 mid punch, +1 on block — your primary frame trap starter and pressure reset tool), 3K (i14 mid kick, -2 on block — safe poke with solid range and good for spacing), 1K (i15 low kick, -5 on block — your main low poke that catches standing opponents off guard), 66K (i17 running knee, +1 on block — approach tool that doubles as a frame trap setup), 41236T (Power Slam — 68 damage command throw from half-circle forward), 63214T (Death Valley Driver — 72 damage command throw with side switch), 360T (Giant Swing — 70 damage 360-degree command throw with massive range), 2T (low throw — 45 damage catch for crouching opponents), 33P (i16 mid punch launcher — primary combo starter), and 8P (i18 launcher — alternative combo starter with slightly more range). For defense, Bayman's 4H mid kick hold leads to a grounded combo, and his armor stance provides a unique defensive option that no other character possesses. The combination of high-damage command throws, plus-on-block pressure, and armor reads makes Bayman terrifying in the hands of a patient player.
Q: How does Bayman's playstyle differ from Tina's in DOA6?
While both are grapplers, Bayman and Tina play fundamentally differently. Tina is an offensive grappler — she wants to rush in, establish strike conditioning, and then throw. Her game plan is proactive: get close, apply frame traps, and force the 50/50. Bayman is a defensive grappler — he wants to sit at mid-range, absorb or bait the opponent's attacks using his armor and superior pokes, then punish their mistakes with devastating command throws. Tina wants to pressure; Bayman wants to counter-pressure. Tina's combos are shorter and more throw-end-focused; Bayman's combos deal more raw damage from strikes alone, making him less throw-dependent in practice. Tina's command throws are mostly half-circle motions; Bayman has the 360-degree Giant Swing which has more range but requires cleaner execution. In terms of matchup dynamics, Tina struggles against fast rushdown characters who don't give her time to condition; Bayman struggles against zoners who refuse to approach because his approach tools are slower. Pick Tina if you like aggressive pressure and reading block habits; pick Bayman if you like patient defense, armor reads, and punishing overcommits.
Q: What are Bayman's best combo routes in DOA6?
Bayman's combo game is built around two launchers: 33P (i16 mid punch) and 8P (i18). The universal BnB is: 33P → 6PP → 4K → 3K for 75 damage. For wall carry, use 33P → 6PP → 66K → 4K → 3K, which carries roughly 65% of the stage. When near a wall, the optimal route is 33P → 6PP → 4K (wall splat) → 6PP → 6T for 90 damage. With Break Blow meter, extend the combo: 33P → 6PP → Break Blow → 66K → 4K (wall splat) → 6PP → 6T for 118 damage. On Counter Hit, the 33P launches higher, allowing you to add an extra 6K before the 6PP: 33P (CH) → 6K → 6PP → 4K → 3K for 88 damage. Bayman's combo enders leave the opponent grounded at his ideal okizeme range, setting up the command throw vortex immediately after every combo.
Q: How do I counter Bayman in DOA6?
Countering Bayman requires understanding that his defensive game is his offense — if you play predictably, you feed his armor and throw setups. The correct approach is: (1) Mix up your timing — Bayman's armor absorbs committed attacks, so use quick jabs and staggered pressure to bait the armor, then punish its recovery with a full combo. (2) Stay at long range — Bayman's approach tools (66K, dash-in throws) are slower than Tina's. Characters with strong keep-out tools (Jann Lee, Hayabusa) can wall him out. (3) Break his command throws — Bayman's 41236T is a 6T break, his 63214T is a 4T break, and his 360T Giant Swing is a 6T break. Learn which throw he favors in each situation and break accordingly. (4) Attack after blocking his minus moves — Bayman's 3K is -2 on block and his 1K is -5. After blocking either, challenge with your fastest mid to interrupt his next action. (5) Do not over-commit to strings — multi-hit strings are Bayman's armor food. Use single-hit confirms and wait for the armor, then launch. Bayman's key weakness is that he thrives on the opponent's predictability — if you stay unpredictable and mobile, he can never set up his reads.
