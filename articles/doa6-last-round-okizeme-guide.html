DOA6 Last Round Okizeme Guide: Wake-Up Pressure, Meaty Attacks & Oki Setups

Every knockdown in DOA6 Last Round is a chance to keep the pressure going — but only if you know how to exploit the wake-up game. Okizeme (oki) is the art of attacking an opponent as they recover from being knocked down, and mastering it is the difference between winning one exchange and winning the entire round. This guide covers every wake-up mechanic, universal oki tools, character-specific setups, and a complete training regimen to make your knockdowns truly terrifying.

What Is Okizeme & Why It Wins Rounds

Okizeme — abbreviated as "oki" by the FGC — literally means "attacking on wake-up" in Japanese. In DOA6 Last Round, every knockdown creates a temporary advantage state where the attacker can pressure the defender before they can return to neutral. The defender must make a choice from a limited set of options, and the attacker's job is to read that choice and punish it.

Here is why oki is one of the most important skills in competitive DOA6:

But if you follow up with oki and deal another 40-60 damage from the wake-up situation, you can close the round from a single opening. This is how tournament players snowball rounds.

Every Wake-Up Option in DOA6: The Complete Breakdown

Before you can pressure wake-ups, you need to understand what the defender can do. DOA6 Last Round has four primary wake-up options, each with different properties, timings, and counters.

Option 1: Stay Down (Do Nothing)

The defender remains on the ground and does not press anything. After approximately 2 seconds, the character automatically stands up with brief wake-up invincibility. Stay down is the safest option against impatient attackers because it avoids all meaty timing windows — if the attacker swings too early, their attack whiffs over the grounded opponent.

Counter: Ground-hit attacks (moves that hit opponents on the floor) force the opponent to take damage or get up. P+K ground slams, 2P low pokes, and specific character ground-hit tools punish stay-down players. If they stay down twice in a row, they eat guaranteed ground damage.

Option 2: Quick Rise (Tech-Roll)

Pressing any attack button as you hit the ground causes your character to roll sideways and stand up roughly 15-20 frames faster than staying down. Quick rise is the fastest recovery option and the default choice for most competitive players because it minimizes the opponent's oki window. However, quick rise has a predictable timing — the opponent always rises at the same frame after teching.

Counter: Meaty attacks timed for the quick-rise window. Since quick rise always takes the same number of frames, you can pre-time a strike to hit on the exact frame the opponent becomes vulnerable. This is the foundation of all DOA6 oki.

Directional quick rise: Holding forward during quick rise makes your character roll toward the opponent; holding back rolls away. Forward quick rise is aggressive but puts you closer to the attacker's pressure; backward quick rise creates distance but gives up stage position.

Option 3: Wake-Up Attack

By holding a direction and pressing P or K while getting up, the defender performs an armored rising attack. Wake-up attacks have super armor during the startup frames, meaning they absorb one hit before striking. They are the primary "get off me" tool — if the attacker is pressing buttons, the wake-up attack absorbs the hit and counters.

Counter: Wake-up attacks are punishable on block (typically -12 to -18 frames). If you expect a wake-up attack, simply block it and punish with a full combo. You can also throw an opponent out of a wake-up attack's startup if the timing is right. The key insight: wake-up attacks beat mindless aggression but lose to patient defense.

Option 4: Wake-Up Hold

Inputting a hold direction (4H, 1H, 7H, etc.) while rising causes the defender to perform a counter as they stand up. Wake-up holds are high-risk, high-reward: if the attacker strikes at the predicted timing and height, the hold catches it for a reversal. If the attacker delays, throws, or uses a different attack height, the hold whiffs and the defender eats a full punish.

Counter: Throws (universally beat holds), delayed attacks (bait the hold and punish recovery), and meaty timing (a correctly timed meaty connects before the hold's active window starts). Wake-up holds are the most common defensive habit online — and the most punishable when read correctly.

The oki game is fundamentally about pattern recognition and adaptation.

Meaty Attacks: Timing Your Strikes for Guaranteed Pressure

A meaty attack is the most important oki tool in DOA6 Last Round. The concept: time your attack so its active hit frames overlap with the opponent's first vulnerable frame as they wake up. If done correctly, the meaty connects before any wake-up hold or wake-up attack can activate, beating three of four wake-up options.

How Meaty Timing Works

Every attack in DOA6 has three phases: startup frames (before the hit becomes active), active frames (the window where the attack can connect), and recovery frames (after the active window ends, where the attacker is vulnerable). A meaty works because the active frames are already in contact with the opponent's hurtbox when their wake-up invincibility expires.

Example: Kasumi's 6P has 9 frames of startup and 3 active frames. If you time it so that the 1st active frame (frame 10 overall) coincides with the opponent's first vulnerable wake-up frame, the attack connects instantly. The opponent's wake-up hold (which needs startup frames of its own) cannot activate in time because your hit is already connecting.

Best Universal Meaty Moves

These moves work as meaties for nearly every character in DOA6 Last Round:

  • 6P (Forward + Punch): Fast (9-11f startup for most characters), mid-hitting, and safe on block. The go-to meaty for every character. Beats quick rise and stay-down, safe if blocked.
  • 2K (Down + Kick): Low-hitting, fast, and forces the opponent to block low on wake-up. Excellent for conditioning — after a few low meaties, the opponent starts blocking low, opening up mid launchers.
  • 66P (Dash Punch): Longer range, forward-moving, catches opponents who try to backdash on wake-up. Slightly slower than 6P but covers more distance.
  • 2P (Down + Punch): Fastest option for most characters (8-9f startup), hits mid, and is safe on block. Use when you need to guarantee the meaty connects.
  • P+K (Special): Many characters have a P+K move with good meaty properties — often a power blow or stance transition that leads to mix-up pressure on hit or block.

Safe Jumps: Risk-Free Wake-Up Attacks

A safe jump is the ultimate oki tool because it beats wake-up attacks while still pressuring the opponent. The technique: jump toward the knocked-down opponent, time your air attack to connect as they rise, then land and block before their wake-up attack can hit you. If they do a wake-up attack, your air attack whiffs or is absorbed, and you land in time to block the armored hit. If they do anything else, your air attack connects.

Safe Jump Setup

  1. Knock down the opponent: Use a specific launcher or throw that leaves them at the correct distance.
  2. Walk forward slightly: Position yourself at roughly 1.5 backdash distances — close enough to jump in, far enough to land safely.
  3. Jump at the right time: Press 9 (forward jump) so your jump arc peaks just as the opponent rises.
  4. Air attack on the way down: Press P or K at the apex of your jump so the active frames connect with the rising opponent.
  5. Land and block: If the opponent did a wake-up attack, you land and immediately hold block. The wake-up attack's startup gives you enough time to recover from landing and block.

Ground-Hit Mix-Ups: Catching Rolls & Stay-Downs

When the opponent chooses to stay down or delays their wake-up, ground-hit attacks force them to take damage or make a decision. Ground hits are moves that strike opponents on the floor, and they are a critical part of oki because they beat the "do nothing" option.

Universal Ground-Hit Tools

  • 2K (Down Kick): Most characters have a 2K that hits grounded opponents. Fast, low-risk, and deals chip damage. Use this to punish stay-down players repeatedly.
  • 3K (Crouch Kick): A slightly slower but more damaging ground hit available to many characters. Good for ending a ground-hit sequence when the opponent is low on health.
  • P+K Ground Slam: Some characters have a P+K move that slams grounded opponents for significant damage. These are slower but deal more damage than basic ground hits.
  • OTG (Off-The-Ground) Throws: Certain command throws in DOA6 can grab opponents who are still on the ground or just rising. These are character-specific and extremely powerful when they exist.

The Ground-Hit Mix-Up

The ground-hit mix-up works like this: after knocking down the opponent, you have a choice between hitting them on the ground (beating stay-down) or waiting for their wake-up and meatying (beating quick rise). The opponent must guess which you will do.

  1. If they stay down: Hit them with 2K for free damage, then prepare for their next wake-up.
  2. If they quick rise: Meaty them with 6P for a combo opportunity.
  3. If they wake-up attack: Block and punish.
  4. If they wake-up hold: Delay slightly, then throw or meaty after the hold whiffs.

This creates a four-way guess on every knockdown, and the attacker has the advantage because they choose their option first while the defender must react.

Quick-Rise Traps: Punishing Tech-Roll Habits

Most competitive players default to quick rise because it is the fastest recovery option. This makes quick-rise traps the highest-value oki investment in ranked and tournament play. If you can consistently punish quick rise, you will dominate the wake-up game against the majority of players.

The Pre-Timed Meaty Trap

The simplest and most effective quick-rise trap: pre-time a meaty attack for the exact frame the opponent quick-rises. Since quick rise always takes the same number of frames after a given knockdown, you can practice the timing once and apply it every time.

Execution: After your knockdown combo, immediately dash forward and press 6P at the correct timing. The timing varies by knockdown type (see the meaty timing table above), but with practice it becomes muscle memory. If the opponent quick-rises, the meaty connects. If they stay down, the attack whiffs harmlessly over them — but you lose nothing.

The Dash-Throw Quick-Rise Trap

After a knockdown, dash forward and throw (5T) at the quick-rise timing. This catches opponents who quick-rise and immediately block (expecting a meaty). The throw beats blocking and also beats wake-up holds — making it an excellent counter to defensive quick-risers.

The Delay-Into-Meaty Trap

Against opponents who quick-rise and then do a wake-up attack, use a slight delay before your meaty. Walk forward 2-3 steps, then press 6P. The delay means your meaty connects after the wake-up attack's armor wears off but before the opponent recovers. This beats wake-up attack habits and quick-rise timing simultaneously.

ADVANCED TRAP: After a knockdown, whiff a light attack on purpose (like 5P) to mask your timing. The whiff animation gives you a natural delay that lines up perfectly with quick-rise timing on many knockdown types. The opponent sees you "miss" and tries to punish, but your follow-up meaty catches their quick-rise recovery.

Throw Okizeme: The Grappler's Secret Weapon

Throws in DOA6 are uniquely powerful for oki because they beat two of the four wake-up options: wake-up holds and blocking. If the opponent quick-rises and immediately blocks (expecting a meaty), a throw catches them. If they wake-up hold, a throw beats the hold. This makes throw oki a devastating complement to strike-based oki.

Universal Throw Oki

Every character in DOA6 can use basic throws (5T and 4T) for oki. The setup: after a knockdown, approach the opponent's wake-up position and throw at the quick-rise timing. If they block, the throw connects. If they hold, the throw connects. You only lose to quick-rise into wake-up attack or backdash.

Command Throw Oki

Characters with command throws (grapplers like Bass, Tina, Bayman, and Leon) have enhanced oki because command throws deal significantly more damage and often lead to additional knockdowns, creating an oki loop.

  • Bass: After 41236T (Giant Swing) knockdown, dash forward and 6H+K (meaty launcher) or 41236T again (command throw). The opponent must guess between the strike and the throw — both lead to 80+ damage.
  • Tina: After 33T (Moonsault) knockdown, walk forward and 6P (meaty) or 6T (throw). Tina's oki is simpler but equally effective because her throws deal massive damage.
  • Bayman: After 214T (Counter Hold throw) knockdown, 6P meaty or 360T command throw. Bayman's armored stance makes his oki particularly oppressive.
  • Leon: After 214T knockdown, 3H+K (ground hit) into throw mix-up. Leon's ground-hit game complements his throw oki for layered pressure.

The Strike/Throw Oki Guess

The most effective oki mix-up is the strike/throw 50/50. After a knockdown:

  1. Option A — Meaty Strike: Time a mid attack for the quick-rise window. Beats wake-up holds and quick-rise into block. Loses to wake-up attack.
  2. Option B — Command Throw: Time a throw for the quick-rise window. Beats blocking and wake-up holds. Loses to wake-up attack and backdash.

The opponent must guess between A and B. If they guess wrong, they eat significant damage. This is the core of grappler oki and the reason characters like Bass and Tina are so dangerous in the right hands.

Hayabusa has the most ambiguous oki in the game thanks to his Izuna teleport. After a knockdown, Hayabusa can teleport to either side of the opponent, creating a left-right mix-up that is extremely difficult to block on reaction. The setup: knockdown → 236P (Izuna teleport) → meaty from the opposite side. The opponent must guess which side Hayabusa will appear on — wrong guess leads to a back-hit combo for massive damage.

Kasumi's teleport dash (66) makes her oki deceptively strong. After a knockdown, Kasumi can dash through the opponent to switch sides, then immediately meaty from behind. Bass Oki Deep Dive

Bass is the king of oki in DOA6 Last Round. After any knockdown, Bass can choose between 6H+K (armored meaty launcher, beats wake-up attacks) and 41236T (Giant Swing command throw, beats blocking and holds). Both options deal 80+ damage and lead to another knockdown, creating an infinite oki loop until the opponent makes the right guess. On stages with walls, Bass's oki becomes nearly inescapable — his Giant Swing carries to the wall for bonus wall combo damage.

The Oki Flowchart: Decision-Making in Real Time

Use this flowchart every time you knock down an opponent. It takes practice to internalize, but once it becomes automatic, your oki game will improve dramatically:

  1. Knock down the opponent.
  2. Did they quick-rise last time?
    • YES → Pre-time a meaty for the quick-rise window. If they have been blocking, switch to throw.
    • NO → They are likely going to quick-rise this time (players alternate). Pre-time the meaty anyway.
    • UNKNOWN → Default to meaty 6P. It is the safest option and beats the most wake-up choices.
  3. Did your meaty connect?
    • YES → You got a hit. Convert into combo if possible. Next knockdown, repeat the same oki — they will likely try a different wake-up option.
    • NO (they stayed down) → Hit them with 2K ground hit. Next knockdown, they will likely quick-rise to avoid the ground damage. Meaty them.
    • NO (they wake-up attacked) → Block and punish. Next knockdown, they will likely stop wake-up attacking. Meaty them.
    • NO (they wake-up held) → Delay and throw, or delay and meaty. Next knockdown, they will likely stop holding. Meaty them.
  4. After 2-3 knockdowns, the opponent's habits become clear. Exploit the pattern until they change, then adapt again.
MENTAL STACK: The oki flowchart is not about finding the "correct" option — it is about making the opponent's decision harder than yours. You choose your oki option before they wake up. They must react to your choice while also worrying about four different possibilities. This asymmetry is why oki is so powerful: the attacker always has the mental advantage.

Training Mode Oki Practice Routine

Use this 15-minute training regimen to build your oki muscle memory:

Exercise 1: Meaty Timing (5 minutes)

  1. Set Training Mode dummy to "Recovery: ON" and "Wake-Up Action: Quick Rise."
  2. Knock down the dummy with your character's most common launcher.
  3. Attempt a meaty 6P at the quick-rise timing.
  4. If the dummy blocks, your timing was too late. If the dummy gets hit, your timing is correct.
  5. Repeat 20 times. Goal: 18/20 correct meaties.

Exercise 2: Safe Jump Practice (5 minutes)

  1. Set dummy to "Wake-Up Action: Attack."
  2. Knock down the dummy, perform your safe jump setup.
  3. If the dummy's wake-up attack is blocked, your safe jump is correct. If you get hit, adjust spacing or timing.
  4. Repeat 20 times. Goal: 18/20 blocked wake-up attacks.

Exercise 3: Oki Flowchart Practice (5 minutes)

  1. Set dummy to "Wake-Up Action: Random."
  2. Knock down the dummy and attempt the appropriate oki based on what they do.
  3. Practice reading the dummy's choice and selecting the correct counter.
  4. Repeat for 10 knockdowns. Goal: Successfully counter 7/10 wake-up options.
ADVANCED DRILL: Record the dummy performing all four wake-up options after a knockdown. Then play back the recording randomly and practice reacting to each option in real time. This simulates match conditions better than any other training method.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is okizeme in DOA6 Last Round?
Okizeme (oki) is the Japanese fighting game term for wake-up pressure — the art of attacking an opponent as they recover from a knockdown. In DOA6 Last Round, okizeme encompasses meaty attacks (moves timed to hit on the first vulnerable frame), safe jumps (jump-ins that land safely if the opponent does a wake-up attack), ground-hit mix-ups (forcing a choice between hitting grounded or standing guard), and quick-rise traps (setups that punish players who tech-roll immediately). Effective oki turns every knockdown into a second guessing game for the defender.
Q: What are the wake-up options in DOA6?
In DOA6 Last Round, knocked-down players have four main recovery options: 1) Stay Down (remain on the ground until forced up after ~2 seconds), 2) Quick Rise (tech-roll by pressing a button as you hit the ground — fastest recovery), 3) Wake-Up Attack (hold a direction and press P or K while getting up — an armored rising attack), and 4) Wake-Up Hold (input a hold while rising — attempts to counter an expected attack). Each option has strengths and weaknesses: Quick Rise is fastest but predictable, Wake-Up Attacks have armor but are punishable on block, and Wake-Up Holds beat specific attacks but lose to throws and different timings.
Q: How do meaty attacks work in DOA6?
A meaty attack in DOA6 is a strike timed so that the active hit frames overlap with the opponent's first vulnerable frame as they wake up. The key mechanic: if your attack's active frames are already in contact with the opponent when their wake-up invincibility ends, the hit is guaranteed. This means even if the opponent inputs a wake-up hold, the meaty connects before the hold's active window begins. Meaty attacks are the foundation of oki because they beat three of four wake-up options (stay down, quick rise, and wake-up holds). Only a perfectly timed wake-up attack with armor can beat a meaty, and even then the attacker can block it if they expect it.
Q: What is a safe jump in DOA6?
A safe jump is an oki technique where you jump toward a knocked-down opponent and time your air attack to connect just as they rise, then land and block before their wake-up attack can hit you. The timing is precise: your jump attack must have enough active frames to catch the opponent's wake-up, but you must also land early enough to block any armored wake-up attack. In DOA6, safe jumps are most effective after launchers that leave the opponent at a specific distance — typically 1-2 backdash distances. Characters with high jump arcs and fast air-to-ground attacks (like Kasumi and Hayabusa) have the easiest safe jump setups.
Q: How do I deal with wake-up holds in DOA6?
Wake-up holds are the most common defensive option at higher levels. To beat them: 1) Use meaty attacks — a properly timed meaty connects before the hold's active frames start, 2) Delay your pressure — pause briefly to bait the hold, then punish the whiffed hold recovery (typically 20-30 frames of vulnerability), 3) Use throws — throws beat holds in DOA's core triangle system, so a command throw on wake-up catches hold-happy opponents, 4) Mix up your timing — alternate between immediate meaties and delayed strikes to keep the opponent guessing. The key is reading your opponent's habits: if they always hold on wake-up, delay and punish; if they always quick rise, meaty them immediately.
Q: Which DOA6 characters have the best okizeme?
The best oki characters in DOA6 Last Round are: Bass (command throws on wake-up create devastating 50/50 with strikes — his 41236T catches holds and his 6H+K is a meaty launcher), Tina (similar grappler oki with 33T command grab and armored wake-up pressure), Hayabusa (Izuna setups allow ambiguous left-right mix-ups on wake-up), Kasumi (teleport dash creates left-right oki ambiguity, fast meaties with 6P), Jann Lee (Dragon Kick forces wake-up reactions from safe distance), and Zack (Ducking stance creates unique high-low mix-ups on oki). Grapplers generally have the strongest oki because their command throws beat wake-up holds, which is the most common defensive option.
Q: Can you quick rise in DOA6 and how does it work?
Yes, quick rise (also called tech-roll) is performed by pressing any attack button (P, K, or H) as your character hits the ground. Quick rise is the fastest wake-up option — your character rolls sideways and stands up roughly 15-20 frames faster than staying down. However, quick rise is predictable in timing, making it vulnerable to meaty attacks and timed oki setups. In DOA6 Last Round, quick rise can be combined with a directional input: holding forward during quick rise makes your character roll toward the opponent, while holding back rolls away. This adds a positional layer to the wake-up game.
