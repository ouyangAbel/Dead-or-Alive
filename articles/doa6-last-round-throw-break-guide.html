DOA6 Last Round Throw Break Guide: Complete Throw Escape & Defensive System

Throws are the most devastating single-hit damage source in DOA6 Last Round — and throw breaks are the only thing standing between you and getting half your life bar deleted by a grappler's command grab. Understanding the throw system is not optional: it is the defensive skill that separates players who die to every 50/50 from those who force opponents back to honest neutral. This guide covers every throw type, break window by tier, character-specific throw data, the triangle system interaction, and a complete training regimen to make your throw defense tournament-ready.

The DOA6 Throw System: Tiers, Types & the Fighting Triangle

DOA6's combat system is built on the Fighting Triangle: Strikes beat Throws, Throws beat Holds, and Holds beat Strikes. Throws occupy the middle position — they defeat defensive holds but lose to offensive strikes. However, unlike many fighting games where throws are a simple rock-paper-scissors option, DOA6 features a layered throw hierarchy with four distinct tiers, each with its own break mechanics, damage profiles, and strategic purpose.

Every throw in DOA6 falls into one of these four categories:

Throw Break Mechanics: Timing, Direction & Windows

At its core, a throw break is simple: press T during the throw's break window. But the details of how this works vary significantly by throw type, and understanding these nuances is what makes your defense consistent.

The Break Window

When a throw connects, a short window opens during which you can press T to escape. You cannot buffer a throw break before the throw animation begins — this prevents option-select throw breaks that would make throws useless. The window starts when the throw's active frames connect and lasts for a specific duration depending on throw tier. If you miss the window, the throw plays out at full damage.

Directional Breaks (Offensive Holds)

Offensive holds (6T and 4T) require directional matching to break. If your opponent uses 6T (forward throw), you must press 4T (back + throw) to escape. If they use 4T, you press 6T. Pressing the wrong direction or neutral T will not break an offensive hold — this is what makes them a powerful mixup tool. At lower ranks, many players default to one direction, making them easy to read and consistently break.

The Throw Break OS (Option Select)

There is one exception to the no-buffer rule: a defensive option select that covers multiple options. By pressing T with a slight delay after blocking a string (approximately 8-12 frames after blockstun ends), you can buffer a throw break that activates only if the opponent throws. If they strike instead, your character will still be in block recovery and the T input is ignored. This technique — called "fuzzy throw breaking" — is an essential defensive tool at intermediate and advanced levels.

Neutral Throws (5T): The Foundation of Throw Offense

Neutral throws are the basic throw every character has — input with 5T from neutral or after movement. They are the fastest throws in the game at 5-7 frames of startup, making them the go-to option for punishing blocked unsafe moves, catching opponents who hold too much, and establishing throw pressure at close range.

Neutral throws deal a baseline 40-50 damage depending on the character, which is modest compared to command throws but significant when you consider the speed. Landing three neutral throws over a round deals 120-150 damage — that is approximately 40-50% of a standard 300 HP life bar — purely from a 5-7 frame move that beats holds.

When to Use Neutral Throws

  • Punishing unsafe moves: If your opponent uses a move that is -7 or worse on block, a 5T neutral throw is a guaranteed punish — throws beat any defensive option except blocking.
  • Calling out holds: If your opponent is hold-happy, a neutral throw will catch them during their hold attempt for Hi-counter damage (150% of base).
  • Wake-up pressure: After a knockdown, run up and threaten 5T. If the opponent wakes up with a hold, you get a free throw. If they wake up blocking, you reset to neutral at advantage.
  • Tick throws: After a blocked light attack (usually -2 to -4), a neutral throw will beat the opponent's attempted jab or throw, and only lose to a faster jab (character-dependent).
PRO TIP: The neutral throw break window is generous — approximately 20 frames (333ms). This means that at mid-to-high level play, neutral throws are often broken on reaction. High-level players use neutral throws primarily as a conditioning tool: show the opponent you will throw their holds, forcing them to stop holding. Once they stop holding, your strike offense opens up. This is the core rhythm of DOA6 offense.

Offensive Holds (6T/4T): Directional Throw Mind Games

Offensive holds are the middle tier of the throw hierarchy — stronger than neutral throws (more damage, directional mixup) but weaker than command throws (less damage, easier to break). Input by pressing 6T (forward throw) or 4T (back throw), offensive holds deal 50-65 base damage and require the opponent to break with the opposite direction.

Why Offensive Holds Are Powerful

The directional requirement transforms a simple throw into a two-way mixup. The opponent must not only react to the throw animation but also correctly guess whether to press 4T or 6T to break. A wrong guess means they eat full damage. At the top level, offensive holds are the preferred throw option for non-grappler characters precisely because the directional mixup adds a layer of mental stack that neutral throws lack.

Character-Specific Offensive Hold Properties

Not all offensive holds are created equal. Some characters have unique properties on their 6T or 4T that change how opponents must approach the break:

  • Kasumi 6T: Teleport throw — fast animation with deceptive startup, difficult to react to the direction.
  • Hayabusa 6T: Launches into an air combo on certain stages. Breaking this throw denies not just the throw damage but also the juggle opportunity.
  • Tina 6T/4T: Both offensive holds can chain into additional throw attempts, creating a throw-loop mixup that pressures even after a successful break.
  • Bass 6T: Repositions the opponent toward the wall — even if broken, Bass gains positional advantage if the opponent was mid-stage.
READING DIRECTIONAL PATTERNS: Most players at intermediate ranks have a clear directional bias — they almost always use either 6T or 4T for offensive holds. Pay attention during the first round: if your opponent uses 6T twice, default your break to 4T. This pattern recognition alone will dramatically increase your throw break success rate.

Command Throws: Grappler Power & High-Damage Execution

Command throws are the apex predators of DOA6's throw system — high-damage, motion-input throws that can delete 40-50% of a life bar on a single correct read. Command throws are the primary win condition for grappler characters (Bass, Tina, Leon, Bayman) and a powerful mixup tool for several other fighters.

Command Throw Properties

Command throws have three defining characteristics that separate them from other throw tiers:

  1. Higher damage: Base damage ranges from 60 to 120+, scaling up to 150+ on Hi-counter. A Hi-counter Bass 63214T deals approximately 50% of a standard life bar in a single move.
  2. Tighter break window: The break window is approximately 10-14 frames — roughly half the neutral throw window. This puts command throw breaks at the edge of human reaction time, making them a prediction-based defense at all but the very highest levels.
  3. Motion input execution: Command throws require directional inputs (e.g., 41236, 63214, 6321478), making them slightly slower to execute than neutral throws. The motion input itself acts as a telegraph — if you see a grappler wiggling their character with repeated circular motions, they are buffering a command throw, and you should prepare to break or strike them out of the startup.

Hi-Counter Throws & Unbreakable Situations

Not all throws can be broken. Understanding when a throw is guaranteed is just as important as knowing how to break the breakable ones, because attempting to break an unbreakable throw wastes mental energy and leaves you vulnerable to real mixups.

Hi-Counter Throws: The 150% Damage Multiplier

A Hi-counter throw occurs when your throw catches the opponent during a hold attempt. This deals 150% of the throw's base damage and — critically — Hi-counter throws cannot be broken. The logic is simple: you tried to hold, which loses to throws, and the game rewards the thrower with guaranteed damage. This is the fundamental punishment for over-using holds in DOA6 and is why high-level players hold sparingly.

Key Hi-counter situations:

  • Active hold frames: If you input a hold and a throw connects during the hold's active window, it is Hi-counter and unbreakable.
  • Hold recovery: There is a brief window after a whiffed hold where throws still register as Hi-counter — approximately 5-8 frames after the hold animation ends.
  • Wake-up holds: Holding on wake-up is extremely dangerous. A well-timed meaty throw will catch the wake-up hold for Hi-counter damage every time.

Combo Throws: Guaranteed Ender Damage

Combo throws are throws that connect during a combo or stun state. Once the initial hit confirms (usually a launcher or stun starter), the combo throw follow-up is guaranteed and unbreakable. These function as combo enders — the damage is lower than command throws (typically 20-40), but the guarantee makes them the optimal ender when available.

Characters with notable combo throws: Hayabusa (air Izuna after launcher), Kasumi (6T air throw ender), Ayane (back-turned throw ender), Rig (stance throw ender), and every grappler (various command throws as combo enders from stun).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a throw break in DOA6 Last Round?
A throw break (also called throw escape or throw tech) is the act of pressing the Throw button (T/H) at the correct timing to escape an opponent's throw attempt. When you successfully break a throw, both characters are pushed apart with no damage dealt. Throw breaks are a core defensive mechanic that prevent opponents from exploiting the throw side of DOA6's fighting triangle (Strikes beat Throws, Throws beat Holds, Holds beat Strikes). The break window varies by throw tier: neutral throws have ~20 frame windows, offensive holds ~15 frames, and command throws a tight ~10-14 frames.
Q: How do I break throws in DOA6?
To break a throw in DOA6 Last Round, press the Throw button (T or H, depending on your control scheme) within the throw break window of the incoming throw. The timing varies by throw type: neutral throws (5T) have a generous ~20-frame break window; command throws (e.g., 41236T) have a tighter ~12-frame window; and offensive holds (6T/4T) have a moderate ~15-frame window. For multi-direction command throws, you must also match the correct direction (e.g., pressing 4T to break an opponent's 6T offensive hold). You cannot buffer a throw break before the throw animation starts — the input must come during the throw's active window.
Q: What are the different types of throws in DOA6?
DOA6 Last Round features four throw tiers: (1) Neutral Throws (5T) — the basic throw every character has, 5-7 frame startup, breakable with any T input, deals 40-50 damage; (2) Offensive Holds (6T/4T) — directional throws that beat holds but are also breakable, 7-10 frame startup, must break with matching direction, deals 50-65 damage; (3) Command Throws (e.g., 41236T, 63214T) — high-damage motion-input throws used primarily by grappler characters, 10-17 frame startup, breakable with T at correct timing, deals 60-120+ damage; (4) Combo Throws — throws that only connect during combos or stun states, unbreakable once the initial hit confirms, used as combo enders for maximum damage.
Q: Can you break command throws in DOA6?
Yes, command throws in DOA6 Last Round are breakable by pressing T during the throw's break window. However, command throws have a much tighter break window (approximately 10-14 frames) compared to neutral throws (~20 frames), making them significantly harder to escape on reaction. Additionally, some command throws are unbreakable in specific situations — for example, Hi-counter throws (throws that catch the opponent during a hold attempt) cannot be broken regardless of tier. This is why high-level players mix up throw types to condition opponents into guessing wrong, and why spacing is the primary defense against grappler command throws rather than relying on reaction breaks.
Q: What is the throw break window in DOA6?
The throw break window in DOA6 varies by throw type. Neutral throws (5T) have the largest window at approximately 20 frames (about 333 milliseconds). Offensive holds (6T/4T) have a moderate window of approximately 15 frames. Command throws (motion-input throws like 41236T) have the tightest window at approximately 10-14 frames depending on the specific throw. These windows begin from the moment the throw connects — you cannot buffer a throw break before the throw animation starts. This means throw breaks are a reaction-based defensive option, though experienced players often predict throws based on opponent patterns and use fuzzy break option selects to cover multiple throw timings.
Q: Which characters have the best throws in DOA6?
The best throw characters in DOA6 Last Round are the grappler archetype: Bass, Tina, Leon, and Bayman. Bass has the highest-damage command throws in the game, with his 41236T (Giant Swing) dealing 80+ base damage and his 63214T dealing over 100 damage on Hi-counter. Tina has excellent offensive holds and the ability to chain throws together. Leon features devastating air throws that can end rounds from 50% health. Bayman combines throws with his unique tank stance for high-damage mixups. Among non-grapplers, Hayabusa's Izuna (41236T) is the most iconic command throw, dealing 72 base damage with a unique cinematic animation and the ability to reposition opponents during combos.
