Why Training Mode Is Your Most Important Tool

Every top DOA6 player — from online warriors to EVO champions — spends more time in Training Mode than in actual matches. Training Mode is where you build the muscle memory that wins rounds. In a fighting game as deep as DOA6 Last Round, with 31 characters, the Triangle System, Critical Burst mechanics, and stage-specific wall combos, there is simply too much to learn through online matches alone.

Here is what separates casual practice from effective lab work:

Casual PracticeEffective Lab Work
Randomly doing combos against a standing dummyPracticing specific confirms from common hit scenarios
Ignoring frame data entirelyChecking advantage/disadvantage on every move used
Never using Action RecordingRecording opponent blockstrings to practice punishes
Playing only with 0 delayAdding input delay to simulate online conditions
Switching characters every 5 minutesDeep-diving one character for an entire session
No specific goalEntering the lab with a clear question to answer
PRO TIP: Before every training session, write down one specific question you want to answer. Examples: "What is my fastest punish after blocking Kasumi's 6P?" or "Can I wall-splat from mid-screen with my BNB combo?" Having a goal turns aimless practice into directed improvement.

Getting Started: Settings and Basic Navigation

To enter Training Mode, go to FIGHT > TRAINING > FREE TRAINING from the main menu. You will be taken to the character select screen where you can pick any of the 31 fighters (all unlocked in Last Round) and any stage. The match will load into a neutral position on your chosen stage with an opponent dummy.

Essential Pause Menu Settings

Press Start/Options to open the Training Mode menu. Here are the critical settings to configure immediately:

SettingRecommendationWhy
Opponent StateStanding / Crouching / JumpingPractice combos on standing, crouching, or airborne opponents
Counter HitON (for combo practice)Most practical combos require counter-hit starters — practice them this way
Critical GaugeFULLPractice Break Blow combos and gauge-dependent routes without waiting
GuardAll Guard / No GuardSwitch between making the opponent block everything or nothing
RecoveryON / OFFTest if combos are guaranteed or can be teched out of

QUICK RESET: Press Touchpad/Select + R3 (or equivalent) to instantly reset both characters to the starting position. This is the single most-used button combination in any training session — build the habit of resetting after every combo attempt instead of waiting for the dummy to stand up.

Mastering the Frame Data Display

DOA6 Last Round has one of the best in-game frame data tools of any 3D fighter. Toggle it on from Pause Menu > Display Settings > Frame Data. Once enabled, every move you perform will show its full frame data in real time on screen.

Here is what each number means and how to use it:

DisplayMeaningHow to Use It
StartupFrames until the move hitsLower = faster. Use to find your fastest punish. DOA6 jabs are typically 10-12f.
ActiveFrames the move can hitLonger active frames = better for catching sidesteps and backdashes
RecoveryFrames you cannot act after the moveHigh recovery moves are unsafe on block — do not whiff them in neutral
Hit Adv.Frame advantage on hitPositive = you act first. Build frame traps around plus-on-hit moves
Block Adv.Frame advantage on blockNegative = opponent acts first. Negative 7 or worse = likely punishable
Counter Adv.Advantage on counter-hitCounter-hits often give enough advantage for guaranteed follow-ups
DRILL: Pick your character's 10 most-used moves (check our Frame Data Guide for reference). Perform each one and note the block advantage. Create a mental list: "These 3 moves are safe (0 to -5), these 4 are slightly unsafe (-6 to -8), and these 3 are launch-punishable (-9 or worse)." This 5-minute exercise will immediately improve your match decision-making.

Combo Practice: From Beginner to Advanced

Combos are the flashiest part of Training Mode, but practicing them correctly is what turns flash into consistency. Follow this progression for every new combo you learn:

Stage 1: Land It Raw (5-10 minutes)

Set the opponent to Standing with Guard: Off and Counter Hit: ON. Practice the full combo route from start to finish. Aim for 10 consecutive successful reps before moving on. If you drop it, the counter resets to zero. This builds execution consistency under zero pressure.

Stage 2: Confirm It (10-15 minutes)

Set the opponent to Guard: Random. Now you must visually confirm whether your starter hit before committing to the full combo. This is the single most important combo skill — hit-confirming separates intermediate players from advanced players. If the opponent blocks, switch to a safe string ender. If it hits, go into your full combo.

Stage 3: Spacing It (10 minutes)

Many combos only work at specific ranges. Set up the dummy at different distances — point-blank, tip range, and max range of your starter. Practice the combo from each position. You will discover that some routes only work up close, while others have better range. Map this knowledge for real matches.

Stage 4: Under Delay (10 minutes)

Turn on the Input Delay Simulator (see section below) to 2-3 frames. Re-run your combo until you can land it consistently with lag. This is the difference between a "training mode combo" and a "tournament combo."

COMBO OPTIMIZATION: After mastering a basic route, experiment with variations. Try ending with a Break Blow instead of a launcher. Test wall-splat extensions. See if you can fit an extra jab before the launcher. Small optimizations add 10-20 damage per combo, which wins rounds.

Hold and Counter Practice: Building Defensive Muscle Memory

The Hold system is DOA's defining defensive mechanic. A player who can consistently land holds turns their opponent's offense into their own damage. Here is how to train your hold reactions systematically:

Phase 1: Single-Attack Hold Practice

Set the opponent to perform a single, predictable attack on repeat using Action Recording. Start with slow, telegraphed moves (20f+ startup) and work down to fast jabs (10-12f). Practice the correct hold direction until you can counter 10 in a row. The hold directions are:

  • High Punch Hold (7H): Counters high punches
  • High Kick Hold (9H): Counters high kicks
  • Mid Punch Hold (4H): Counters mid punches
  • Mid Kick Hold (6H): Counters mid kicks
  • Low Punch Hold (1H): Counters low punches
  • Low Kick Hold (3H): Counters low kicks

Phase 2: String Hold Practice

Record the opponent performing a 2-3 hit string. For example, Kasumi's 6PK. Practice holding the final hit of the string rather than mashing hold during the blockstun. The key is timing — you cannot hold while in blockstun, so you must wait for the gap. The frame data display will show you exactly when that gap occurs.

Phase 3: Mix-Up Hold Practice

Record 2-3 different attacks into different Action Recording slots. Set the opponent to play a random slot on reset. Now you must visually identify the incoming attack and choose the correct hold direction. Start with two options (e.g., Mid Punch vs Mid Kick) and gradually add more.

ADVANCED TIP: Expert players use holds not just for defense but as offensive reads. In Training Mode, practice the sequence: knock down opponent → dash in → read their wake-up option → hold on prediction. The most common wake-up attacks to hold are Mid Kick (6H) and Mid Punch (4H).

Action Recording: Program Your Opponent

Action Recording is the single most powerful feature in DOA6 Training Mode — and the most underused. It lets you program the opponent dummy to perform specific sequences, which you then practice defending against or punishing.

Access it from Pause Menu > Opponent Settings > Action Recording. You get 5 recording slots that can be assigned to different situations:

SlotExample RecordingPractice Goal
Slot 1Opponent's BNB blockstringPractice blocking the full string and punishing the final unsafe move
Slot 2Opponent's wake-up kickPractice baiting and punishing wake-up options
Slot 3Opponent's sidestep attackPractice tracking moves that catch sidesteps
Slot 4Opponent's throw attemptPractice throw-breaking on reaction
Slot 5Random Slot 1-3Defensive reads against mixed options

How to record: Select a slot, press Record, and perform the sequence with the opponent character. Press Stop when done. Set Playback to Repeat (loops continuously) or On Reset (plays once per reset). For advanced training, set multiple slots to Random Playback so the opponent mixes between them.

Input Delay Simulator: Practice for Online Play

Offline DOA6 runs with minimal input delay (approximately 1-2 frames depending on platform). Online play adds 1-6 frames of additional delay depending on connection quality. The Input Delay Simulator bridges this gap — and it is the feature most responsible for the difference between players who are "good in training mode" and players who are good in ranked matches.

Enable it from Pause Menu > Display Settings > Input Delay. Set it to 2-3 frames for typical online conditions, or 4-5 frames for poor connections. Then re-run your combos and hold practice with the delay active.

ROUTINE: Spend the first 10 minutes of every session with 0 delay (building clean execution), then 10 minutes with 2 frames of delay (simulating good online), then 5 minutes with 4 frames (simulating bad connections). This progressive approach builds adaptability across all online conditions.

Matchup-Specific Lab Work

When you struggle against a specific character in ranked, take that character into Training Mode as the opponent dummy. Here is a targeted 15-minute lab session template for any matchup:

  1. Learn their strings (5 min): Pick the problem character and perform their 5 most common strings. Watch the frame data. Identify which hits are high (duckable), which leave gaps (holdable), and which end unsafe (punishable).
  2. Practice punishing (5 min): Record the opponent doing their unsafe string. Block it and practice your optimal punish. If the string has a high, practice ducking and launching it.
  3. Test your options (5 min): Set the opponent to guard all. Test which of YOUR moves beat their common approaches. Can you sidestep their pressure starter? Does your 6P beat their approach tool?

Advanced Tools: Critical State, Break Gauge, Stage Hazards

Training Mode also lets you manipulate DOA6's more advanced systems for targeted practice:

Critical State Practice

From the Pause Menu, you can set the opponent's Critical Stun Level manually. Use this to practice Critical Burst combos and test how many hits you can land before the opponent can hold out. Set Critical Stun to Level 3 (max before Burst) and test your longest possible combo routes.

Break Gauge Management

Set Break Gauge to FULL to practice Break Blow and Break Hold scenarios without waiting for the gauge to fill naturally. Practice the Break Blow cancel timing — the window is tight and requires dedicated repetition.

Stage Hazard and Wall Carry

Different stages have different wall distances, danger zones, and breakable floors. Select a stage and practice carrying your opponent to the wall. Measure how many hits it takes from center stage to reach the wall. On stages with breakable floors (like "The Throwdown"), practice your post-floor-break combo extension.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How do I access Training Mode in DOA6 Last Round?
From the main menu, select FIGHT > TRAINING > FREE TRAINING. You can choose any character (including all DLC fighters), any stage, and customize the opponent's behavior. Training Mode loads in about 5-10 seconds on all platforms including Switch 2.
Q: Can I see frame data in DOA6 Training Mode?
Yes. DOA6 Last Round includes a full frame data display that you can toggle from the Pause Menu > Display Settings. It shows startup frames, active frames, recovery, hit advantage, block advantage, and counter-hit advantage for every move in real time as you perform them. This is one of the most comprehensive in-game frame data tools in any 3D fighting game.
Q: How do I record the opponent's actions for practice?
Pause the game and go to Opponent Settings > Action Recording. You can record up to 5 slots of opponent behavior. Record the sequence you want to practice against (e.g., a specific blockstring or wake-up option), assign it to a slot, and set it to play back on loop or on reset. You can also set the opponent to perform a random action from multiple slots for reaction training.
Q: How do I practice hold counters in DOA6 Training Mode?
Set the opponent to perform specific strikes (high/mid/low punch or kick) on repeat using Action Recording. Start with single predictable attacks, then progress to random 2-3 hit strings. Use the frame data display to verify timing windows. For advanced practice, record the opponent doing a full blockstring with a mix-up ender and practice hold-reacting to the final hit.
Q: What are the best Training Mode settings for combo practice?
Set Counter Hit to ON (most practical combos require counter-hit starters), set Critical Gauge to FULL so you can practice Break Blow combos freely, enable Input Display to see your button timing, and turn on Damage Display to track optimization. For wall combos, select stages with close wall proximity like "The Throwdown" or "Zero" for efficient wall-carry practice.
Q: Does DOA6 Training Mode help with online play?
Absolutely. Use the Input Delay Simulator (Pause Menu > Display Settings) to add 1-4 frames of artificial delay, simulating real online conditions. Practice your combos and reaction timings with 2-3 frames of delay to build muscle memory that works online. This is the single most overlooked training setting that separates online warriors from offline-only players.
