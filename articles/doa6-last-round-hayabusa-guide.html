DOA6 Last Round Hayabusa Guide: Combos, Izuna, Frame Data & Complete Strategy

Ryu Hayabusa — the Super Ninja, the Dragon lineage heir, the most iconic fighting game ninja of all time — is one of the deepest, most expressive, and most technically demanding characters in Dead or Alive 6 Last Round. Unlike the straightforward strike-throw game plans of Hitomi or Jann Lee, Hayabusa operates on a completely different axis: he does not just hit you — he vanishes, reappears behind you, teleports through your offense, and punishes your hesitation with the legendary Izuna Drop. This guide covers everything from his fundamental frame traps to advanced Ongyoin stance-cancel mind games, providing the complete path from your first 41236T input to tournament-ready Hayabusa play.

Hayabusa Overview: Playstyle, Strengths & Weaknesses

Hayabusa is a technical rushdown-mix-up hybrid who thrives on unpredictability. His game plan is built around three core systems: the Izuna throw (high-damage command grab that punishes over-defensive opponents), the Ongyoin stance (nine options branching from a single stance entry, creating layered 50/50 situations), and teleport-based movement (33P and air teleports that bypass traditional footsies and create ambiguous left/right mix-ups).

Strengths

  • Unmatched mix-up depth: Ongyoin stance gives Hayabusa nine distinct options from a single entry point. No other character in DOA6 has this many branching threats from one stance.
  • Izuna Drop: 80 damage from a command throw that punishes holding. Against opponents who rely on holds for defense, the Izuna is a match-winning tool.
  • Teleport mind games: 33P and air teleports create left/right mix-ups that most players below A-Rank simply cannot defend against consistently.
  • Strong wall carry: Hayabusa's combo routes carry corner-to-corner on most stages, and his wall-splat options (4H+K) lead to devastating damage.
  • Expert holds: Unique hold animations with combo follow-ups — 7H (high punch hold) and 4H (mid kick hold) lead directly into launcher state.

Weaknesses

  • Poor tracking: Hayabusa's main pokes (6P, 7K, 3K) have very little tracking. Sidestep beats his entire neutral game, forcing him to commit to slower tracking moves or stance entries.
  • Stubby normals: At very close range, Hayabusa's fastest buttons are range-limited. Characters with long-range jabs (Ayane, Christie) can out-poke him in scramble situations.
  • Stance vulnerability: Ongyoin stance has no armor or invincibility frames. If the opponent reads your stance entry, a fast mid poke will knock you out of it before any option can activate.
  • High execution demands: Dropping an Izuna input (41236T) leaves you vulnerable; mistiming a teleport cancel gives the opponent a free punish. Hayabusa demands clean execution under pressure.
PRO TIP: Hayabusa's biggest weakness is predictability. New Hayabusa players tend to repeat the same Ongyoin → K launcher sequence or teleport → Izuna pattern. Against good players, you must constantly rotate your options — show one or two Ongyoin follow-ups, then cancel out of stance entirely to keep the opponent guessing. A Hayabusa who never commits to the same option twice is terrifying.

The 6P Frame Trap: Hayabusa's Bread and Butter

6P is +2 on block. This means after your 6P is blocked, your next button will come out 2 frames faster than the opponent's fastest normal. The basic frame trap sequence is:

  1. 6P (blocked) → 6P: Your second 6P will beat any 10-frame or slower move. If the opponent presses a button after blocking your first 6P, they get counter-hit. This is the simplest frame trap in Hayabusa's arsenal and should be your default pressure tool.
  2. 6P (blocked) → 41236T (Izuna): If the opponent respects the frame trap and blocks, buffer the Izuna. They are locked in blockstun and must hold or try to throw-break — if they hold, the Izuna lands for 80 damage.
  3. 6P (blocked) → 214P (Ongyoin): If the opponent starts fuzzy-guarding or delay-teching, transition into Ongyoin stance. The stance entry itself forces the opponent to guess which of nine options is coming — and guessing wrong against Hayabusa hurts.
  4. 6P (blocked) → slight delay → 6P: If the opponent starts pressing buttons with frame-perfect timing, add a micro-delay. The delay makes your second 6P come out 1-2 frames later, catching opponents who try to disrespect the frame trap on the exact frame.
DRILL THIS: Set the Training Mode dummy to "Guard All" and "Attack after Guarding" with a 10-frame mid. Practice the 6P → 6P frame trap 50 times. You should counter-hit the dummy on every successful repetition. If the dummy hits you, your timing is too slow. This single drill will improve your Hayabusa pressure more than any combo practice.

The Izuna Drop: Input, Variants & Practical Setups

The Izuna Drop (41236T) is Hayabusa's signature move and the single highest-damage command throw in DOA6. At 80 base damage, it punishes the most common defensive habit in the game — holding — and forces opponents into a lose-lose situation against his frame traps.

Mastering the Input

The half-circle forward motion (41236) is not intuitive for most players. Here is the step-by-step breakdown:

  1. Start at 4 (back): Press and hold the directional input toward the back direction. This is the most common starting position because you are often blocking before throwing.
  2. Roll to 1 (down-back): Without lifting your thumb/stick, slide from 4 to 1 in one smooth motion.
  3. Roll to 2 (down): Continue the slide from 1 to 2.
  4. Roll to 3 (down-forward): Continue sliding from 2 to 3.
  5. Finish at 6 (forward) + T: Complete the half-circle by sliding to 6, and press Throw (T) on the same frame you reach 6.

The most common input error is pressing T too early (before reaching 6) or too late (after the half-circle window closes). To practice, go into Training Mode with input display turned on and watch your directional inputs. The display should show a clean 4→1→2→3→6 curve, not a jagged 4→6 shortcut. Clean half-circle = consistent Izuna.

Practical Izuna Setups

The Izuna is not a move you throw out randomly — it must be set up. Here are the three most reliable Izuna setups that work at every level of play:

  1. The Frame Trap Setup: 6P (blocked) → Izuna. This is your bread-and-butter setup. After 6P is blocked at +2, the opponent has three choices: press a button (they lose to a second 6P), block (they get Izuna'd if they are holding), or attempt a throw break. Most opponents will hold after blocking 6P because they expect the frame trap — and holding is exactly what Izuna punishes.
  2. The Ongyoin Conditioning Setup: Enter Ongyoin (214P) and use 2K (low kick) 3-4 times. The opponent will start crouch-blocking to avoid the low. On the next Ongyoin entry, immediately do 4T (Ongyoin Izuna). The opponent is crouching and holding down — the Izuna catches them for 90 damage.
  3. The Knockdown Setup: After a hard knockdown (7K or 236K ender), dash forward and input 41236T as the opponent wakes up. If they wake up holding (which most players do against Hayabusa because they fear his Okizeme pressure), the Izuna connects. If they tech-roll, you are plus enough to start pressure. If they wake-up kick, your 41236 input can be canceled into block by holding back at the end.
THE HI-COUNTER IZUNA: The single most damaging non-combo interaction in DOA6 is a Hi-Counter Izuna — 120 damage, which is roughly 40% of a standard life bar. This happens when you throw an opponent during their hold recovery frames. The setup: after any advantage situation (6P on block, a stun from 3K, etc.), delay your Izuna input by roughly 15 frames. If the opponent panic-holds during your delay, your Izuna will land during their hold recovery and trigger Hi-Counter. Practice this timing — it is the difference between an 80-damage Izuna and a round-ending 120-damage Izuna.

When you enter Ongyoin, Hayabusa crouches into a low profile, and the opponent must immediately guess which of nine different options is coming. The stance itself has no defensive properties — no armor, no parry — so it is purely an offensive commitment. But the branching threat tree is so wide that even opponents who know every option must guess correctly multiple times per interaction.

The Three-Layer Ongyoin Offense

Hayabusa's Ongyoin offense operates in three layers that you cycle through based on opponent adaptation:

Layer 1 — The Basic Mix-Up (2K / K): The simplest and most reliable Ongyoin offense. Show the opponent 2K (low) a few times. Once they are conditioned to crouch, mix in K (mid launcher). This is a true high/low mix-up — the opponent must guess correctly. This layer alone will carry you through F-Rank to C-Rank.

Layer 2 — The Throw Layer (T / 4T): When opponents start blocking the 2K/K mix-up consistently, introduce throws. After 3-4 Ongyoin entries where you only showed strikes, on the next entry immediately throw (T or 4T). The opponent is conditioned to block — the throw punishes their passivity. The 4T Ongyoin Izuna does 90 damage, which is more than most launcher combos.

Layer 3 — The Cancel Layer (66 Teleport Cancel): When opponents start aggressively interrupting your Ongyoin entry (which is the correct counter-play), use the teleport cancel (66 from Ongyoin). This causes Hayabusa to dash out of stance before any option triggers, baiting the opponent's interrupt attempt. If they whiff a button expecting you to commit, you are now at advantage and can punish. The teleport cancel turns their correct defensive read into a whiff punish opportunity for you.

THE GOLDEN RULE OF ONGYOIN: Never show the same option three times in a row. If you do 2K twice, the opponent will start crouching. If you do K twice, they will start standing. If you do T twice, they will start mashing. Cycle your options constantly. The goal is not to land every single Ongyoin entry — it is to make the opponent so uncertain that they freeze, giving you free damage on every subsequent entry.

Teleport Mechanics: 33P, Air Teleports & Ambiguous Cross-Ups

Hayabusa's teleport (33P) is one of the most misunderstood tools in DOA6. On paper, it is a slow (i27), unsafe (-8 on block) move that seems like a gimmick. In practice, it is a position-reversing, cross-up-creating, mental-stack-overloading tool that forces the opponent to track an additional axis of defense — and most players simply cannot handle it.

33P Teleport: How It Works

When you input 33P, Hayabusa vanishes in a puff of smoke and reappears behind the opponent with a punching attack. The key property: regardless of where the opponent is facing or what they are doing, the teleport always places Hayabusa behind them. This means:

  • On block: The opponent must switch their guard direction from facing forward to facing backward. If they do not react fast enough, the punch connects.
  • On hit: Hayabusa is at +12 and the opponent must now defend against a cross-up follow-up — except they are facing the wrong direction.
  • During the opponent's recovery: If you teleport while the opponent is recovering from a whiffed move or in blockstun, they literally cannot turn around fast enough to block it.

Practical Teleport Setups

  1. Whiff Punish Teleport: When the opponent whiffs a slow move (especially linear moves like Jann Lee's Dragon Kick or Bass's charged attacks), immediately 33P. The recovery frames prevent them from blocking the cross-up.
  2. Oki Teleport: After a hard knockdown, dash forward and 33P as the opponent wakes up. If they stand-block forward (expecting frontal pressure), the teleport hits their back. If they wake-up kick, the teleport's high-crush frames (frames 9-18) go under mid kicks.
  3. Pressure Reset Teleport: After a blocked 6P (at +2), instead of frame-trapping with another 6P, use 33P. The opponent expects frontal pressure and holds or blocks forward — the teleport crosses them up.
  4. Air Teleport Cancel: After a launcher (8K), input 9 → 44 during the jump to air-teleport cancel instead of committing to a juggle. This is a hard read tool — if the opponent expected a juggle and held, you land beside them with advantage. If they didn't hold, you gave up guaranteed damage, so use this sparingly.
THE MENTAL STACK ATTACK: Every time you teleport, you force the opponent to process: (1) where is Hayabusa now, (2) do I need to switch my guard direction, (3) is he going to strike or throw when he appears. This adds roughly 200-300 milliseconds of mental processing time — time during which the opponent is vulnerable to whatever you do next. The teleport's real value is not the 33P damage; it is the mental fatigue it creates, making the opponent slower to react to your real mix-ups.

Optimal Combo Routes: BnB, Wall Carry, Break Blow & Counter Hit

Hayabusa's combo game is built around three launchers — 8K (fastest), 214P,K (Ongyoin launcher), and 3H+K (low launcher) — with optimized routes for every situation.

Universal BnB (Normal Hit, Midscreen)

Route: 8K → 6KP → PP4PK

Damage: 68

This is your go-to combo. It works from any 8K launcher at any range, has consistent timing, and the PP4PK ender gives solid knockdown advantage. Practice this until you can execute it with your eyes closed — it is the foundation of Hayabusa's entire combo game.

Counter Hit / Hi-Counter Hit Extension

Route: 8K (Counter Hit) → 4KK → 6K → PP4PK

Damage: 82 (CH) / 98 (HCH)

On Counter Hit, the 8K launches higher, giving you time for an extended route. The 4KK re-launches, 6K juggles, and PP4PK ends. This is the route you use when you score a counter-hit confirm — the damage difference between this and the BnB is 14-30 points, which adds up over a match.

Optimal Wall Carry

Route: 8K → 66K → PPP → PP4PK

Damage: 72 (carries ~60% of stage length)

When you are mid-stage and want to push the opponent to the wall, replace the 6KP juggle with 66K into PPP. The 66K carries farther, and PPP being a long string helps push the opponent across the stage. On stages with close walls (The Throwdown, Lost Paradise), this combo will wall-splat from almost any starting position.

Wall Splat Combo

Route: (Near wall) 4H+K (Wall Splat) → 66 → PP4PK

Damage: 78 (no launcher needed — 4H+K alone wall-splats)

4H+K is Hayabusa's dedicated wall-splat tool. At i20, it is slow but has excellent range and active frames. The micro-dash (66) after the wall splat aligns Hayabusa for the PP4PK ender. If the wall splat is high (opponent bounced off the upper wall), you can substitute the ender with 6KP → PP4PK for additional damage (85 total).

Break Blow Extended Combo

Route: 8K → 6KP → Break Blow → 4H+K (Wall Splat) → PP4PK

Damage: 110 (midscreen), 125 (near wall)

This is your maximum-damage route when you have full Break Gauge. The Break Blow must be input immediately after 6KP connects — any delay and the opponent will tech out. The Break Blow's knockback carries the opponent to the wall (even from mid-stage), and 4H+K converts the wall splat into a full combo. At 110-125 damage, this combo turns a single launcher into roughly 40-45% of a standard life bar.

Ongyoin Launcher Combo

Route: 214P → K (launch) → 6KP → PP4PK

Damage: 72

The Ongyoin mid kick launcher (K) is slightly slower than 8K but has better range and is more ambiguous because the opponent must also consider the low (2K) option. The combo route is identical to the standard BnB after the launch. The real value of this combo is the threat — every time you enter Ongyoin, the opponent knows K = 72 damage, which forces them to consider crouching (and eating the 2K or T mix-ups instead).

Neutral & Spacing: Pokes, Whiff Punishment & The Mid-Range Game

Hayabusa's neutral game is built around controlling the mid-range with safe, long-reaching pokes and baiting the opponent into whiffing so you can punish with 8K. He lacks the long-range neutral tools of characters like Ayane or Jann Lee, so Hayabusa players must be clever about how they navigate the space between full-screen and close range.

The Mid-Range Toolkit

At mid-range (roughly 2-3 character widths apart), Hayabusa has three primary tools:

  • 7K (i14, -4 on block): This is your best keep-out tool. At -4 on block, it is safe against everything except i9-i10 jabs, and even those trade with spacing. Use 7K to stuff the opponent's approach when they dash in or try to close distance with a long-range move. The knockdown on hit gives you okizeme pressure.
  • 236K (i18, -7 on block): A spinning mid kick that covers distance quickly. The true strength of 236K is the 236K4 variant — the 4 at the end transitions into Ongyoin stance mid-spin, putting Hayabusa directly in the opponent's face with stance options ready. If the kick is blocked, you are in Ongyoin at close range. If it hits, you can choose to combo or continue stance pressure.
  • 3H+K (i28, -14 on block): The slide kick. Very slow, very unsafe, but it crushes highs starting at frame 9 and launches on hit. Use this when the opponent is committed to high attacks or when they are approaching too predictably. The risk is significant — if blocked, you eat a full combo — so use it as a hard read, not a neutral poke.

Whiff Punishment

Hayabusa's 8K launcher at i13 is one of the best whiff-punish tools in the game. The key to whiff punishing with Hayabusa is spacing yourself just outside the opponent's longest-range button. Against Hitomi, stay outside 6PK range. Against Jann Lee, stay outside Dragon Kick range. When they throw out their longest button and miss, dash forward → 8K → full combo. This single skill — spacing for whiff-punish — is what separates good Hayabusa players from great ones, because it allows you to launch punish from a position where the opponent thought they were safe.

THE BAIT-AND-PUNISH LOOP: At mid-range, throw out one 7K. If it is blocked, walk backward slightly. The opponent, seeing you back away, will try to close distance with a long-range move. You have walked backward to make that move whiff — and now you are in range to 8K punish. This loop — safe poke → micro-backwalk → whiff punish — is the core of Hayabusa's mid-range game. Practice it until the rhythm is automatic.

Matchup Strategy: How to Beat Every Archetype

Hayabusa's approach changes dramatically based on the opponent's character archetype. Here is the high-level game plan against each of the four major DOA6 archetypes.

vs. Rushdown (Kasumi, Christie, Hitomi, Jann Lee)

Rushdown characters want to be in your face, pressing plus-on-block buttons and forcing you into defensive guessing games. Hayabusa's counter-strategy relies on 7K keep-out and teleport escapes. Use 7K at max range to stuff their approach — at -4, you are safe even if blocked, and the knockdown gives you breathing room. When they do get in and start their pressure, do NOT try to contest with buttons. Instead, look for gaps to teleport out: 33P when they pause between strings, or air teleport cancel after blocking a launcher attempt. The goal against rushdown is to never let them establish sustained close-range pressure — keep resetting to mid-range where your 7K and 8K whiff-punish dominate.

vs. Grapplers (Bass, Tina, Bayman, Leon)

Grapplers want you to block — the moment you hold block, they start their command throw vortex. Against grapplers, Hayabusa's game plan is simple: do not block more than necessary, and abuse the Izuna. Grappler offense relies on you freezing up in fear of their throws. But Hayabusa's throw game is just as dangerous — and faster. After their blocked strings (most of which are negative), immediately challenge with 6P. If they try to command throw, your i11 6P beats their i12 throw startup. If they block your 6P, the +2 advantage sets up the Izuna mix-up — and grapplers are especially vulnerable to the Izuna because their entire game plan conditions them to hold less (they want to throw instead). Against a grappler who is throwing a lot, Izuna on their throw startup trades in your favor — 80 damage > 60 damage.

vs. Setplay/Zoners (Ayane, Nyotengu, Phase 4, Marie Rose)

Setplay characters rely on creating space with long-range attacks and then forcing you to navigate their trap setups. Ayane spins away; Nyotengu takes to the air; Marie Rose uses her kleine schwert stances. Hayabusa's answer is 33P teleport to bypass their space control entirely. When Ayane does her back-spin to create distance, 33P teleports directly behind her. When Nyotengu enters flight stance, 33P appears behind her and punishes her landing recovery. The teleport ignores their space-control tools because it does not travel through the space — it removes Hayabusa from the screen and places him behind them. Against setplay characters, use teleport aggressively to deny them the distance they need to function.

vs. Hybrid/All-Rounders (Rig, Diego, Kokoro, Brad Wong)

Hybrid characters do a bit of everything — they have solid pokes, decent throws, and footsie tools. Against these characters, Hayabusa should play his standard layered offense: mid-range 7K poking into Ongyoin stance entries, 6P frame traps into Izuna threats, and occasional teleport cross-ups to test their defense. The key against hybrids is patience — they do not have a single overwhelming strength that you must shut down, so they win by exploiting your impatience. Play solid, stay unpredictable, and trust that Hayabusa's mix-up depth will create openings over time. Do not force the Izuna or the teleport — let them come naturally from your frame-trap pressure.

The Hayabusa Learning Path: Beginner to Master

Learning Hayabusa is a long-term commitment. Here is a structured progression that takes you from your first 41236T to competitive-ready Hayabusa play across four distinct phases.

Phase 1: The Fundamentals (Hours 1-10)

Goals: Consistent Izuna input. BnB combo (8K → 6KP → PP4PK) on both sides. 6P frame trap (6P on block → 6P counter-hit).

Practice Routine: 15 minutes of Izuna input drills (aim for 90% consistency). 10 minutes of BnB combo on both sides (aim for 10 in a row without dropping). 5 minutes of 6P frame trap against Training Mode dummy. Then play 5-10 casual matches, focusing only on landing the Izuna and the BnB. Do not worry about winning — the goal is execution under live pressure.

Rank Expectation: F-Rank to D-Rank. At this level, the Izuna alone will win matches because opponents hold constantly and do not know how to avoid command throws.

Phase 2: The Stance Game (Hours 10-30)

Goals: Ongyoin 2K/K mix-up. 236K4 stance transition. Counter-hit confirm into extended combo. Basic teleport usage (whiff punish and oki).

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of Ongyoin option drills (cycle through P, K, 2K, T, 4T — drill until each option is instant from 214P). 10 minutes of 236K4 into Ongyoin follow-ups. 5 minutes of counter-hit confirm practice (set dummy to random counter-hit). Then 10 ranked matches, with the goal of entering Ongyoin at least 5 times per match and using at least 3 different options.

Rank Expectation: D-Rank to C+. Opponents at this level can block basic strings but cannot handle the Ongyoin 2K/K mix-up consistently.

Phase 3: The Teleport Game (Hours 30-60)

Goals: Teleport cross-up setups (blockstring, oki, whiff punish). Teleport cancel from Ongyoin (66). Air teleport cancel. Wall carry optimization. Matchup-specific game plans for your 5 most common opponents.

Practice Routine: 10 minutes of teleport setup drills (dummy on random guard — practice 33P after blocked 6P, after knockdown, after whiffed 8K). 10 minutes of wall carry combo practice on the 5 most common competitive stages. 5 minutes of matchup-specific labbing (record the dummy doing your problem matchup's top string and practice your counterplay). Then ranked grind with a focus on teleport usage — aim to use at least 3 teleports per match.

Rank Expectation: C+ to B+. Teleport cross-ups destroy players at this level because their mental stack cannot handle the directional confusion.

Phase 4: The Complete Hayabusa (Hours 60+)

Goals: Full stance-cancel mind games. All 9 Ongyoin options used situationally. Hi-Counter Izuna setups. Optimal Break Blow routing. Complete matchup knowledge against the full roster.

Practice Routine: This phase is about integration. Your daily routine shifts to 70% ranked matches and 30% labbing — because at this level, the lab work is matchup-specific (labbing the one character you lost to yesterday) rather than general execution drills. After every ranked session, note the one character who gave you the most trouble and spend 15 minutes in Training Mode labbing their pressure strings and finding your punish windows.

Rank Expectation: B+ to S-Rank. A complete Hayabusa at this level has become one of the hardest characters in DOA6 to defend against, because you have removed every predictable pattern from your game and replaced it with true option-tree ambiguity.

THE HAYABUSA MINDSET: Hayabusa rewards patience and punishes greed. The players who struggle with Hayabusa are the ones who try to force the Izuna every interaction, or teleport constantly without setup, or enter Ongyoin at unsafe ranges. The players who succeed with Hayabusa play him like a chess game — every teleport, every Izuna, every stance entry is a response to the opponent's demonstrated tendencies, not a gamble. Read first, act second. Your offense only works when it is unpredictable — and unpredictability comes from observation, not randomization.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How do you perform the Izuna throw with Hayabusa in DOA6?
The Izuna throw (also called the Izuna Drop) is Hayabusa's signature command throw executed with 41236T (half-circle forward + Throw). The Izuna does massive damage (80 base damage) and has a unique animation that makes it one of the most iconic moves in fighting games. Hayabusa also has an air Izuna variant (33T on an airborne opponent) and enhanced Izuna options from Ongyoin stance (214P,4T). Q: How do I use Hayabusa's Ongyoin stance effectively?
Ongyoin stance (214P) is Hayabusa's most powerful offensive tool, offering nine different follow-ups: P (fast mid punch combo starter), K (mid kick launcher), 2K (low kick for high/low mix-up), T (command throw), 4T (Izuna from stance — extremely high damage), 8P (jumping punch to beat crouch attempts), H (defensive hold against strikes), P+K (Break Blow shortcut), and 66 (teleport dash cancel to reset pressure). Q: What are Hayabusa's best combo routes in DOA6?
Hayabusa's combo game revolves around three launchers: 8K (standing launcher, i13, great for whiff punishment), 214P,K (mid kick launcher from Ongyoin), and 3H+K (low launcher for high/low mix-ups — very slow but rewarding). Q: How do I counter Hayabusa in DOA6?
Countering Hayabusa requires understanding his three main offensive layers. First, his teleport (33P): the teleport always places Hayabusa behind you — fuzzy guard by holding back immediately after seeing the teleport animation. Second, his Ongyoin stance: the stance has no inherent invincibility — if you see Hayabusa enter Ongyoin at close range, use a fast mid poke (i11-i12) to stuff his options. Third, his Izuna throw: the half-circle input takes roughly 20 frames to execute, so if you see Hayabusa dashing into throw range without attacking, he is likely buffering Izuna — strike him during the input window. His key weaknesses are: poor tracking on his main pokes (sidestep beats 6P and 7K), limited range on his fastest buttons, and a reliance on stance transitions that can be interrupted by aggressive play. The best strategy against Hayabusa is to stay close, pressure him with fast mids, and never let him establish Ongyoin stance at mid-range.
