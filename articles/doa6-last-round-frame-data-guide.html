What Is Frame Data in DOA6?

In fighting games, every action is measured in frames. DOA6 Last Round runs at 60 frames per second, which means one frame equals approximately 16.67 milliseconds. Every punch, kick, throw, hold, and movement is broken down into a sequence of frames that determine how fast a move starts, how long it stays active, and how long the attacker needs to recover before they can act again.

Frame data is not just abstract theory. It is the concrete, measurable reason why you sometimes get hit after blocking an attack, why certain combos are guaranteed, and why experienced players seem to take their turn at exactly the right moment. When you understand frame data, you stop guessing and start making informed decisions.

Think of frame data as the physics engine of competitive play. Just as understanding gravity helps you predict how a ball will fall, understanding frame data helps you predict who gets to act next after any exchange. It turns every encounter from a coin flip into a calculated risk.

How to Read Frame Data

Frame data for any move in DOA6 is expressed as three core values, sometimes written in shorthand notation like i12 / -5 / +3:

  • Startup (i-number): The number of frames before the move becomes active and can hit the opponent. An i12 move means it takes 12 frames from the input to reach the active hitbox. Lower is faster.
  • On Block (+/-): The frame advantage or disadvantage when the opponent blocks the attack. A move that is -5 on block means the attacker is 5 frames behind the defender and must wait longer before acting. A move that is +3 on block means the attacker recovers first and has a 3-frame advantage.
  • On Hit (+/-): The frame advantage or disadvantage when the attack lands. Moves that are +5 or more on hit typically guarantee a follow-up attack.

Some frame data charts also list active frames (the window during which the move can connect) and recovery frames (the time after the active window before the attacker can move again). For practical purposes, startup and advantage on block/hit are the most immediately useful values.

Pro Tip: In DOA6 Training Mode, enable the Frame Advantage display under Display Settings. This shows you the exact advantage or disadvantage of every move in real time, so you do not need to memorize every number before you start applying frame data concepts.

Key Frame Data Terms Explained

Startup Frames (i-frames)

Startup frames count from the first frame of the attack input to the first active hit frame. When players say a move is "i12", they mean it has 12 frames of startup. The lower this number, the harder the move is to react to. Most character jabs (standing P) are between i8 and i10, making them the fastest normals in the game.

Active Frames

Active frames are the window during which the move can actually hit the opponent. A move with more active frames has a larger timing window to connect. This matters for meaty attacks on wakeup, where you time a move so its active frames overlap with the opponent's recovery from a knockdown.

Recovery Frames

Recovery is the period after the active frames where the attacker cannot block or perform another action. Longer recovery means more vulnerability. Heavy attacks and whiffed moves typically have long recovery, which is why whiff punishing is so powerful in DOA6.

Frame Advantage

When a move is +4 on block, the attacker recovers 4 frames before the defender can act. If both players press their fastest button immediately, the attacker's move will land first because of this 4-frame head start. Frame advantage is the foundation of pressure, mix-ups, and guaranteed follow-ups.

Frame Disadvantage

When a move is -7 on block, the defender recovers 7 frames before the attacker. The defender now has the advantage and can start their own pressure. A move that is -10 or worse on block is considered punishable because most characters have i10 or i12 launchers that can fit into that window.

Guaranteed Combos

A guaranteed combo occurs when the frame advantage on hit is large enough that the next attack in the sequence connects before the opponent can block, hold, or sidestep. These are not true combos in the combo counter sense, but they are mathematically inescapable. Frame data tells you exactly which follow-ups are guaranteed.

Safe vs Unsafe Moves

Understanding the safe/unsafe threshold is the single most impactful frame data concept for intermediate players.

The Safety Threshold

A move is generally considered safe if it is -5 or better on block (meaning -5, -4, -3, -2, -1, 0, or positive). At -5, the opponent's fastest attack (an i8 jab) would need to start within 3 frames of you recovering, which is not enough for most punishes.

A move is unsafe if it is -6 or worse on block. At -6, fast characters with i8 jabs can begin to interrupt. At -10, most standing P punishes become viable. At -12, many characters can launch you for a full combo. At -15 or worse, nearly every character has a devastating launcher punish available.

Safety Tiers

  • +1 to +5 on block: Advantageous. Your turn continues. Pressure with fast mix-ups.
  • 0 to -5 on block: Safe. The opponent can try to take their turn, but you are not at significant risk.
  • -6 to -9 on block: Risky. Fast characters can poke you. Varies by matchup.
  • -10 to -12 on block: Punishable. Most characters can land a jab punish or small combo.
  • -13 or worse on block: Launch punishable. Full combo opportunity for the opponent. Use very sparingly.

Key Insight: In DOA6, the throw break system means even safe moves carry some risk. If you repeatedly use the same safe string, the opponent can read the pattern and throw you during the gap. Frame safety does not mean pattern safety.

Punishment Guide: How to Punish Everything

Punishing is the act of hitting your opponent during their recovery from an unsafe move. Frame data tells you exactly which moves you can punish and with what. This is the fastest way to gain an edge in any match.

Step 1: Identify the Opponent's Unsafe Moves

Every character has a handful of commonly used moves that are punishable on block. When you are learning a matchup, go into Training Mode, set the CPU to the character you struggle against, and record them using their key moves. Practice blocking each one and noting which of your moves can reach them during recovery.

Step 2: Build a Punishment Flowchart

Organize your punishes by speed tier so you always have the right answer ready:

  • i8-i10 punish (jab or 2P): For lightly minus moves (-6 to -9). Quick damage, resets to neutral.
  • i12 punish (fast mid): For moderately unsafe moves (-10 to -12). Often leads to a small combo or knockdown.
  • i14-i16 launcher: For heavily unsafe moves (-13 to -15). Full combo opportunity.
  • i16+ power punish: For extremely unsafe moves (-16 or worse). Maximum damage starter.

Step 3: Punish Whiffs

Not all punishment happens on block. When an opponent whiffs (misses) an attack entirely, their recovery is usually even longer than on block. Learn to recognize the startup animations of common whiffed moves and respond with your highest-damage starter. This is called whiff punishing and it is one of the most effective strategies in DOA6 competitive play.

Understanding Advantage and Disadvantage

Frame advantage is not just about punishment. It shapes the entire flow of every exchange.

When You Are at Advantage (+frames)

If you are at +3 after a blocked move, your next i10 attack will land on the same frame as the opponent's i13 attack. This means your fast options beat their fast options. In practice, being at advantage lets you apply pressure: mixing between fast mids, throws, and delay timings to keep the opponent guessing.

When You Are at Disadvantage (-frames)

If you are at -4 after a blocked move, the opponent's i10 attack trades with your i14 attack. At -7, their i10 beats your i17. The more minus you are, the fewer options you have. At heavy disadvantage, your only choices are to block, use a hold (if you can read the incoming attack), or try to sidestep.

The Mental Game of Frames

Here is where DOA6 gets interesting: the Triangle System adds a layer on top of pure frame data. Even when you are at disadvantage, a well-timed hold can beat the opponent's strike. Even when you are at advantage, the opponent can use a hold to call out your predictable pressure. Frame data tells you who should win the exchange, but the Triangle System means there is always a counter to every option.

The best players use frame data to narrow their decision tree, not to eliminate all risk. If you are +5, you know strikes are favored. But if the opponent has shown a pattern of holding, you might throw instead. Frame data plus reads equals dominance.

How to Practice Frame Data in Training Mode

Theory is useless without practice. Here is a step-by-step routine to internalize frame data and make it second nature.

Setup: Enable Frame Display

Enter Training Mode, open the pause menu, go to Display Settings, and enable Command Input Display and Frame Advantage Display. The command input shows your notations, and the frame advantage display shows the exact +/- frames after every blocked or hit move.

Routine 1: Map Your Key Moves (10 minutes)

Go through your main character's top 15 most-used moves. Hit the dummy with each one and note the on-hit and on-block advantage. Write them down or take a screenshot. Focus on identifying which moves are safe, which are punishable, and which leave you at advantage.

Routine 2: Build Your Punishment List (10 minutes)

Switch the dummy to record mode. Record the opponent doing a move that is -10 on block (consult a frame data sheet). Block it, then try punishes with i10, i12, and i14 moves. Repeat for -12, -15, and -18 moves. This builds muscle memory for converting blocked unsafe moves into damage.

Routine 3: Pressure Strings (10 minutes)

Practice your character's key advantage strings. For example, if your character has a string that ends at +3 on block, practice following it with a fast mid or throw. Then practice the same string but delay the follow-up to catch opponents who try to hold. This trains you to use advantage for mix-ups rather than auto-piloting.

Routine 4: Matchup Punishment (10 minutes)

Pick the character you struggle against most. Record their 3 to 5 most commonly used moves. Block each one and find your optimal punish. Repeat until you can punish on reaction every time. This single routine will win you more matches than any combo practice.

Daily Habit: Spend 15 minutes per day on frame data practice. After two weeks, you will notice a dramatic improvement in your ability to take your turn, punish mistakes, and avoid getting blown up by unsafe options.

Character-Specific Frame Data Notes

Every character in DOA6 Last Round has unique frame data that shapes their game plan. Here are some general patterns to keep in mind when studying your character's specific data:

Fast Characters (Kasumi, Christie, Hitomi)

These characters have access to i8 jabs and fast strings that keep opponents locked down. Their game plan revolves around maintaining frame advantage through quick sequences and mixing in throws when opponents respect the pressure. Their weakness is usually lower individual hit damage, so they need more successful exchanges to win.

Heavy Hitters (Bass, Bayman, Leon)

Power characters often have slower startup on their key moves but deal massive damage per hit. Their frame data tends to be more minus on block, meaning they rely more on reads, holds, and the threat of their powerful throws. When they land a hit, the frame advantage usually guarantees devastating follow-ups.

All-Rounders (Hayabusa, Jann Lee, Rig)

These characters have balanced frame data with a mix of fast pokes and powerful mid-range tools. Their frame data rewards versatility: fast jabs for interrupting, safe mids for poking, and threatening launchers for punishes. Learning their full frame data suite gives you an answer for every situation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is frame data in Dead or Alive 6?
Frame data refers to the numerical values that describe how fast each move starts up, how long it is active, how much recovery it has, and whether the attacker is at advantage or disadvantage on hit or block. It is measured in frames, where 60 frames equals one second.
How do I check frame data in DOA6 Last Round?
In Training Mode, go to Display Settings and enable the Command Input and Frame Advantage display. This shows the frame advantage or disadvantage of every move you perform on hit and on block in real time.
What does +5 on block mean in DOA6?
+5 on block means the attacker recovers 5 frames before the defender. The attacker can start a move that takes 5 fewer frames to land than the defender's fastest option, giving them a guaranteed advantage in the next exchange.
What is the fastest move in DOA6 Last Round?
Standing jab (P) for most characters comes out in 8 to 10 frames, making it the fastest normal attack. Some characters have i7 or i8 specific moves, and holds are typically instant or 1-frame startup.
How do I punish unsafe moves in DOA6?
When your opponent uses a move that is minus on block (negative frames), identify their recovery window, then use a fast enough starter to hit them before they can block again. A -12 move means any attack starting in 12 frames or fewer will punish it. Practice specific punishes in Training Mode against each character.
Does DOA6 have the same frame data as DOA5?
No. DOA6 has a significantly different frame data system from DOA5. Moves have been rebalanced, new mechanics like the Break Gauge affect frame properties, and several characters have new or modified moves. Always use DOA6-specific frame data.
