Dead or Alive 6 Last Round Best Characters for Beginners: How to Choose Your Main

Picking your first character in DOA6 Last Round can make or break your experience. With 30+ fighters spanning wildly different playstyles — from lightning-fast ninjas to methodical grapplers — the wrong choice can leave you frustrated before you even learn the fundamentals. This guide ranks the most beginner-friendly characters, explains what makes each one easy to learn, and gives you a clear framework for choosing a main that matches how you actually want to play.

Why Your First Character Matters

In a roster as large as DOA6 Last Round, your first character shapes everything: which mechanics you learn first, how you approach neutral, what defensive habits you build, and most importantly — whether you stick with the game long enough to reach the fun part.

Pick a character with overly complex stance transitions like Brad Wong or Helena, and you will spend your first 20 hours fighting the character instead of the opponent. Pick a character with simple, effective tools like Hitomi or Jann Lee, and you can focus on learning the Triangle System, movement, and match flow — the skills that actually transfer between characters.

This does not mean you must stick with your first character forever. Many top players started with a beginner-friendly pick before transitioning to more technical mains. The goal is to learn the game on a character that rewards you quickly, then branch out once the fundamentals are automatic.

What Makes a Good Beginner Character in DOA6

After analyzing the entire roster through a beginner lens, we identified five criteria that separate the best starter characters from the rest:

  1. Simple, Reliable BnB Combos — The character has bread-and-butter combos that work from common launchers without requiring strict timing or stance cancels. You should be able to land 80+ damage within your first hour.
  2. Safe Neutral Tools — Moves you can throw out in neutral without getting launched for it. A fast mid poke (12-14 frames), a safe launcher, and a tracking move to catch sidesteps cover 90% of situations.
  3. Forgiving Frame Data — Most key moves are safe on block (-6 or better). You will not eat a full launch punish every time your string gets blocked.
  4. Clear Game Plan — The character has an obvious win condition that does not require deep matchup knowledge. You know what you are supposed to do: get in, apply pressure, land the launcher, run the combo.
  5. Transferable Skills — Playing this character teaches fundamentals that apply across the roster: spacing, whiff punishing, strike/throw mix-ups, hold prediction. You are not learning a gimmick that only works on one character.

Top 5 Best Beginner Characters (Ranked)

1. Hitomi — The Perfect Starter Character

Difficulty: Very Easy | Style: Karate (Balanced All-Rounder)

Hitomi is widely considered the best beginner character in DOA6 Last Round, and for good reason. Her karate-based moveset is built around forward momentum, simple strings, and a straightforward game plan: get in and punch them.

Why beginners should play Hitomi:

  • Best BnB combo in the game for beginners: 6P+K (launch) → P+K → 4P → PPP does 92 damage with zero execution requirements.
  • 6P is one of the best neutral pokes in DOA6 — 12-frame mid with good range, safe on block, and leads into all of her best pressure strings.
  • Strong whiff punisher with 236P — a lunging punch that punishes opponents who swing in the open. This alone teaches you the most important intermediate skill: spacing.
  • Her hold game is forgiving — 46H is a universal parry that works against all mid punches, giving you a defensive panic button while you learn proper hold timing.
  • Remains tournament-viable at high levels. You will never outgrow Hitomi; Japanese and Korean tournaments regularly feature Hitomi players in top 8.

2. Jann Lee — Aggressive Rushdown Made Simple

Difficulty: Easy | Style: Jeet Kune Do (Rushdown / Pressure)

Jann Lee is the textbook rushdown character: high speed, high damage, high reward for pressing buttons. If your instinct is to always be the one attacking, Jann Lee rewards that aggression more consistently than any other character at the beginner level.

Why beginners should play Jann Lee:

  • Dragon Gunner (66T) is the best throw in the game — 10-frame throw with massive range that beats holds and leads to a guaranteed combo. It single-handedly teaches opponents to stop holding predictably.
  • 66K is a safe, advancing mid kick that closes distance and leaves you plus on block. Press 66K → PP to start your pressure. Simple and effective.
  • His launchers are fast and intuitive: 9K (high crush launcher), 4K (mid launcher), and 3K4K (delayable mid string into launch) give you mix-ups at every range.
  • 236K teaches whiff punishing naturally — a long-range flying kick that punishes missed attacks from half-screen. Landing this consistently is your first real skill check.
  • High damage ceiling without execution barriers. Jann Lee combos do 90-110 damage with basic routes; optimized routes push 130+.

3. Kasumi — Speed and Evasion for Reactive Players

Difficulty: Easy | Style: Mugen Tenshin Ninjutsu (Speed / Evasion)

Kasumi is the fastest character in DOA6 Last Round. Her moveset rewards players who like to dart in, land quick hits, and evade before the opponent can respond. If you prefer being untouchable over being tanky, Kasumi is your pick.

Why beginners should play Kasumi:

  • Fastest jabs in the game (9 frames) — you beat virtually every other character at close range. PP into 6PK is a safe, simple string that opens opponents up.
  • Her teleport (7P) teaches evasion fundamentals — a built-in sidestep that avoids linear attacks and repositions you behind the opponent. Learning when to teleport and when to block is the core Kasumi skill.
  • Excellent low crush options with 9K and 33K, letting you jump over low pokes and launch for a full combo.
  • Her strings are easy to confirm: PP6PK is a natural combo that knocks down and gives you okizeme. PP6P2K adds a low option for a simple high-low mix-up.
  • Forces you to learn the Triangle System properly — Kasumi has low base damage, so you cannot win by mashing. You must land throws against holding opponents and use holds to stop pressure, making her an excellent teacher of fundamentals.

4. Diego — Street Brawler with High Damage, Low Complexity

Difficulty: Very Easy | Style: Street Fighting (Brawler / Power)

Diego was literally designed by Team Ninja to be the onboarding character for DOA6. His moveset has fewer moves than any other character, every string is simple and effective, and his damage output is enormous. If you want to skip the menu and start winning matches immediately, Diego delivers.

Why beginners should play Diego:

  • Smallest move list in the game — you can learn every Diego move in under 30 minutes. No stance systems, no stance cancels, no complex inputs.
  • His tackle (66T) is a 50/50 reset tool that puts the opponent in a guessing game with huge damage potential. Land the tackle, choose punch or kick follow-up, and watch their health disappear.
  • 236P is a long-range armor move that absorbs one hit and counter-hits for a launch. It teaches opponents to stop pressing buttons at range — a lesson they learn the hard way.
  • Simple wall carry: any launcher into PPPP carries opponents to the wall where Diego's damage spikes even higher.
  • Teaches strike/throw mix-up fundamentals — when opponents start holding your predictable strings, you switch to throws. When they stop holding, you go back to strikes. Diego makes this cycle crystal clear.

5. Tina — The Grappler That Teaches Mind Games

Difficulty: Medium | Style: Professional Wrestling (Grappler)

Tina is the best beginner grappler in DOA6 Last Round. Unlike Bass (who requires complex throw chains) or Bayman (who relies on hold-based offense), Tina has fast, high-damage command throws with simple inputs and a solid strike game to back them up.

Why beginners should play Tina:

  • 41236T (Giant Swing) is a 10-frame command throw with enormous range that does 70+ damage on normal hit. Land this twice and any opponent will start mashing — which is exactly what you want.
  • 6H+K is an excellent mid-range poke that gives Tina a neutral tool beyond just walking forward and throwing. It leads to a mix-up on hit.
  • Wall pressure is her win condition: carry to wall with throws, then use running throw (66T) as a terrifying mix-up tool. Opponents at the wall against Tina are in the most dangerous position in DOA6.
  • Teaches throw-strike conditioning: when they hold expecting your throw, hit them with 3P launcher. When they block expecting the launcher, throw them. Tina teaches the fighting game mind game better than any other character.
  • 4H (low hold) is one of the best defensive tools — it beats low pokes and low launchers that new players struggle to react to.

Tina is rated Medium difficulty not because her inputs are hard, but because grapplers require you to read the opponent. You cannot win by flowcharting strings; you must observe when they hold, when they mash, and when they freeze. This makes her the best teacher of fighting game psychology on the roster.

Honorable Mentions: Solid Alternatives

Rig — The Taekwondo Rushdown

Rig is a strong alternative for beginners who want a more technical rushdown character than Diego. His stance transitions (Bending Stance) add a layer of complexity but his core tools — 6P, 4KK launcher, 3K low crush — are intuitive and effective. If Diego feels too simple and Jann Lee does not click, try Rig.

Mila — MMA Hybrid

Mila blends striking and grappling in a way that teaches complete DOA6 gameplay. Her takedown (66T) leads to mounted punches, her 3P is a solid launcher, and her overall game plan — strike until they block, tackle when they freeze — teaches offensive flow better than most characters. Slightly harder than the top 5 but more rewarding long-term.

Leifang — The Defensive Specialist

Leifang is the best defensive character for beginners who prefer counter-punching. Her sabaki parries (46P, 1P+K) automatically counter specific attacks, removing the need for precise hold timing. She teaches patience and whiff punishing — wait for the opponent to make a mistake, then make them pay.

Characters Beginners Should Avoid

Some DOA6 characters are tournament monsters in the right hands but terrible learning tools at the beginner level. These characters share one or more of the following problems: complex stance systems, execution-heavy combos, poor neutral tools, or game plans that teach bad habits.

Brad Wong — Stance Overload

Brad has four stances (Dokuritsu-ho, Dokuritsu-ho 2, Handstand, Laying Down) that interweave in complex ways. His best combos require stance cancels mid-sequence. As a beginner, you will spend more time in the command list than in actual matches. Master him later; avoid him now.

Helena — The Stance Queen

Helena's Bokuho stance is her entire offense, and every transition into and out of it requires matchup-specific knowledge. Without understanding frame data and opponent tendencies, Helena becomes a character where you press buttons and hope something works. Her low damage without optimized routes makes losses feel especially punishing.

Hayabusa — Execution Heavy

Hayabusa is one of the best characters in DOA6 at the tournament level, but his optimal play requires Izuna holds (41236H on high/mid punches) with strict timing and advanced combo routes that involve teleport cancels. His neutral game relies heavily on spacing and whiff punishing — skills beginners have not developed yet. Pick him up after 100+ hours on a simpler character.

Alpha-152 — Gimmick Character

Alpha-152 has a unique float-heavy moveset that plays nothing like any other character. The skills you learn on Alpha do not transfer, and her extreme weight class (lightest in the game) means you die to combos that other characters survive. She is a specialist pocket pick, not a beginner main.

How to Test Characters Before Committing

You do not need to pick a main on day one. Here is a testing routine that takes about 90 minutes total and prevents the most common beginner mistake: spending 30 hours on the wrong character.

Step 1: Command Training (10 min per character)

Go into Training Mode, open the command list, and run through the character's moves. Pay attention to how the inputs feel. Do the strings flow naturally? Do the launchers make sense? If you are fighting the inputs, the character is not for you.

Step 2: Combo Challenge (10 min per character)

Look up the character's basic BnB combo on the Combo Guides page. Practice it for 10 minutes. If you cannot land it at least 5 times in a row by the end of that session, the character's execution barrier may be too high for now.

Step 3: Arcade Mode on Normal (15 min per character)

Run through Arcade Mode on Normal difficulty. This tests whether the character feels good in actual match flow, not just in the lab. Do their tools work against a moving opponent? Does your game plan hold up when someone is hitting back?

Step 4: Three Casual Online Matches

Take the character into casual online matches. Win or lose, the question is: did you have fun? Did the character feel like an extension of your intentions, or were you fighting them the whole time?

Test at least three characters using this routine before committing to one. The character that feels right in the lab often feels wrong in a match, and vice versa.

When to Switch Characters: From Beginner to Advanced

You will know it is time to branch out when the following signs appear:

  • You win matches without thinking about your inputs. Your BnB combos, neutral pokes, and defensive reactions are automatic. This means you have freed up mental bandwidth for higher-level strategy.
  • You feel limited by your character's options. Maybe Hitomi's limited low game frustrates you, or Diego's lack of range makes certain matchups feel impossible. This is not a sign that your character is bad — it is a sign that you have learned enough to identify gaps.
  • You are curious about a specific advanced character. You keep watching Hayabusa combo videos or getting destroyed by a Christie player and thinking "I want to do that." Curiosity is the best reason to switch.

When you do switch, keep your beginner character as a secondary. Having a comfort pick for unfamiliar matchups reduces the frustration of learning a new character from scratch.

5 Common Mistakes When Choosing a Main

  1. Picking based on tier list placement. Tier lists reflect tournament-level optimization against the entire roster. At beginner and intermediate ranks, player skill overwhelms tier differences. A C-tier character played by someone who knows their frames will destroy an S-tier character played by someone who does not. Pick who you enjoy, not who tops the charts.
  2. Switching characters every time you lose. Every character has bad matchups. Losing to a specific opponent does not mean your character is wrong; it means you need to learn that matchup. Stick with one character for at least 50 hours before deciding they are not for you.
  3. Copying pro players without understanding why. Watching a pro player dominate with Eliot does not mean Eliot is a good beginner pick. Pros have thousands of hours of fundamentals under their belt. They make hard characters look easy because their fundamentals are already elite.
  4. Ignoring playstyle fit. If you hate blocking and love rushing in, do not pick a defensive character just because someone told you Leifang is a good beginner pick. You will get bored, tilt, and quit. Pick the character that matches how you actually want to play.
  5. Not using Training Mode before committing. Spending 20 hours on a character you have only seen in YouTube videos is a recipe for buyer's remorse. Always lab a character for at least 30 minutes before investing serious time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the single best character for a complete beginner in DOA6 Last Round?
Hitomi. She has the simplest effective BnB combos, the best neutral poke in 6P, forgiving frame data on her key moves, and a game plan that teaches every fundamental at the same time. She also remains tournament-viable, so you never need to switch unless you want to.
Should beginners play grapplers in DOA6?
Tina is the only grappler recommended for beginners. Her command throws have simple inputs (41236T, 63214T), she has solid strike tools to fall back on, and she teaches the strike/throw mix-up mind game better than any other character. Bass, Bayman, and Lisa require more advanced throw-chain knowledge and are better saved for intermediate play.
How many characters should I learn as a beginner?
One. Pick a single character and commit to them for your first 50-100 hours. Learning one character deeply — their frame data, optimal combos, matchup knowledge — teaches you more about DOA6 than playing five characters at a surface level. After you have fundamentals on your main, learning a secondary takes a fraction of the time.
Is Kasumi too hard for beginners?
No. Kasumi is actually one of the best beginner picks. Her inputs are simple, her speed means you get to press buttons more often, and her teleport teaches evasion fundamentals. The concern is that her low damage might frustrate players who want big combos immediately, but for players willing to accept slightly lower damage in exchange for speed and safety, Kasumi is excellent.
When should I switch off my beginner character?
Switch when your inputs are automatic and you feel limited by your character's toolkit — typically after 50-100 hours. At that point, you have built transferable fundamentals and can appreciate what a more complex character offers. Keep your beginner character as a secondary for unfamiliar matchups.
Does the beginner character I pick affect my long-term growth?
No — as long as you pick from the recommended list. Hitomi, Jann Lee, Kasumi, Diego, and Tina all teach transferable DOA6 fundamentals. What matters is that you stick with one character long enough to build muscle memory and game sense. A player who spends 100 hours on Diego and then switches to Hayabusa will be far better than someone who spent 20 hours each on five different characters.
Can I reach high ranks with these beginner characters?
Absolutely. Hitomi, Jann Lee, and Kasumi all appear regularly in tournament top 8s. Diego and Tina have dedicated high-level mains who prove the characters work at every rank. The player matters infinitely more than the character at all but the top 0.1% of competitive play.
What if none of the top 5 feel right?
Try the Honorable Mentions: Rig (technical rushdown), Mila (MMA hybrid), or Leifang (defensive counter-puncher). If none of those click either, visit the full Roster page and read through the character profiles. Sometimes a character just speaks to you visually or thematically, and enjoyment is the most important factor in whether you stick with the game.
